Genovese A, Hegstrom L, Prabha S, et al. Artificial Authority: The Promise and Perils of LLM Judges in Healthcare. Bioengineering (Basel). 2026;13(1):108. doi:10.3390/bioengineering13010108
https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12837895/
Overview: Narrative review mapping the LLM-as-judge landscape in healthcare AI. Catalogues three dominant architectures — G-EVAL, Prometheus 2, Panel-of-LLM Evaluators — and surveys applications in summarization, QA, and therapeutic conversations. Central finding: judge reliability tracks task objectivity. Judges approach or exceed clinician agreement on objective dimensions (factuality, grammaticality) but degrade on subjective ones (empathy, harm perception). Task: cross-task methodology synthesis. Clinical setting: summarization, QA, conversations. Signal: both explicit and interpretive.
Eval question: When does LLM-as-judge substitute for human review, and what architectural or methodological choices control the reliability ceiling?
Methodology: Structured narrative review. Organizes literature by judge architecture (G-EVAL=CoT prompting; Prometheus 2=fine-tuned judge; Panel=consensus of diverse judges) and by task. Compares validation approaches across studies. No new empirical data.
Measurement: Review-level. Identifies structural reliability patterns: unit decomposition + chain-of-thought prompting both improve per-criterion agreement.
Key findings: Objective criteria (factuality, grammaticality): LLM judges match or exceed inter-clinician agreement. Subjective criteria (empathy, harm perception): substantial degradation, not reliable stand-alone. Decomposition + CoT consistently improve both. Most field evidence is still preprint. Imbalanced training data creates blind spots for rare failure modes.
Soft spots: Narrative (non-systematic) review — selection bias possible. Bioengineering (MDPI) is broad-scope, not clinical-AI specialist. No new primary data or head-to-head architecture comparison. 'Approaches or exceeds inter-clinician agreement' is ceiling-effect sensitive.
Strengths: First structured synthesis of the three dominant judge architectures. Explicit objective-vs-subjective reliability gradient is a clear teaching frame. Names structural patterns (decomposition, CoT) that are directly actionable. Peer-reviewed in a field still dominated by preprints. Honest about training-data blind spots.
Relevance: Layer 1: Cross-task — LLM-as-judge architectural synthesis. Layer 2: Extends KTP #8 (LLM-as-judge validated but bounded) with architectural taxonomy. Relevant to Consultation Quality kit (interpretive → expect lower reliability), Nuclear Medicine kit (factual → expect high reliability). Provides a ready frame for kit-level decisions on whether LLM-as-judge is viable. Complements PQ-100 (VeriFact ceiling), PQ-78 (reasoning eval), PQ-109 (calibrated jury).
Verdict reasoning: Peer-reviewed synthesis that gives /evaluate and the Guidance library a ready frame for LLM-as-judge architecture selection and realistic reliability ceilings. Objective-vs-subjective gradient is consistent with our existing evidence. Structural-reliability patterns (decomposition, CoT) match Nuclear Medicine kit's existing approach. MDPI is mid-tier but the contribution is organizational, not empirical — synthesis quality is the relevant criterion.

Liu D, Long Y, Zuoqiu S, et al. Reliability of Large Language Model Generated Clinical Reasoning in Assisted Reproductive Technology: Blinded Comparative Evaluation Study. J Med Internet Res. 2026;28:e85206. doi:10.2196/85206
https://www.jmir.org/2026/1/e85206/
Overview: Blinded comparative evaluation of LLM-generated clinical chains-of-thought (CoTs) on 200 real ART cases — IVF 70%, ICSI 19%, PGT 11%. Three prompt strategies compared head-to-head: zero-shot, random few-shot, selective few-shot. Two board-certified reproductive physicians (>10 yrs each) scored independently on 5-point Likert across Logical Coherence and Clarity (LCC), Use and Coverage of Key Information (UCKI), Plausibility and Clinical Accuracy of Reasoning (PCAR), after a pre-calibration session. Critical finding: selective few-shot significantly outperformed both alternatives — but GPT-4o as AI evaluator rated all three conditions within a 0.04 Likert range (3.96-4.00), failing to detect the clinically meaningful differences humans identified.
Eval question: Can LLM-as-judge reliably discriminate between prompt strategies on clinical reasoning quality, or does it smooth over clinically meaningful differences?
Methodology: 200 real ART cases across IVF/ICSI/PGT. Three prompt strategies compared. Two board-certified physicians (>10 yrs) independently blinded-scored on 5-point Likert across three dimensions (LCC, UCKI, PCAR), with pre-calibration session. GPT-4o scored in parallel as AI evaluator for comparison. Linear weighted kappa planned but ceiling effects invalidated it — authors report adjacent-agreement (88-100%) instead.
Measurement: 5-point Likert per dimension. Adjacent-agreement rates (88-100%); disagreement rates (0-12%). Target: selective > random > zero-shot on all three dimensions.
Key findings: Zero-shot: LCC 4.18, UCKI 4.30, PCAR 3.85. Random few-shot: LCC 4.31, UCKI 4.42, PCAR 3.91 (NOT significantly different from zero-shot on PCAR, p>0.05). Selective few-shot: LCC 4.56, UCKI 4.66, PCAR 4.18 — significantly better on all three. AI evaluator (GPT-4o): 3.96-4.00 across all conditions (range 0.04), failed to detect the differences humans caught. Selective strategy's edge attributed to 'gold-standard depth' + 'representative diversity' in example selection.
Soft spots: Single specialty (reproductive medicine). 200 cases is moderate; Likert ceilings limit discrimination. Only two raters — minimal for inter-rater reliability on a 5-point scale. Selective few-shot requires expert-curated examples (labor cost not quantified). GPT-4o as sole AI evaluator — newer/specialized judges may differ.
Strengths: Real clinical cases. Pre-calibration session AND head-to-head of three prompt strategies — rare. AI-evaluator-vs-human comparison with a clear failure mode makes the finding quantitatively sharp. Adjacent-agreement reporting (instead of ceiling-capped κ) is methodologically honest. Directly testable implication: pre-validate the judge before adopting it for interpretive A/Bs.
Relevance: Layer 1: Generation, clinical reasoning, prompt optimization, LLM-as-judge discrimination. Layer 2: Strong confirmation of KTP #8 (bounded). Adds a testable protocol to Prompt_Optimization_Guide.md: before using LLM-as-judge to compare prompt variants, run a human-vs-judge discrimination test. Relevant to Consultation Quality and Nuclear Medicine kits. Directly relevant to /optimize — prompt strategy comparisons should use human-in-the-loop during A/B, not rely on LLM-as-judge alone.
Verdict reasoning: Peer-reviewed JMIR, real clinical cases, head-to-head with a clear failure mode. The AI-evaluator-failure finding (GPT-4o range 0.04 across strategies humans clearly differentiated) is a clean, operationalizable constraint on LLM-as-judge use. Selective few-shot result also useful for prompt_interventions_by_task.md. Single-specialty and single-judge-model limitations don't undercut the core methodology contribution.
PQ-112 Interesting, verify
Gupta M, Kumar D. Diagnosing LLM Judge Reliability: Conformal Prediction Sets and Transitivity Violations. arXiv:2604.15302 [cs.AI]. 2026. https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2604.15302
https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.15302
Overview: Methods paper proposing two per-instance LLM-judge reliability diagnostics: (1) split conformal prediction over a 1-5 Likert scale yielding prediction sets with (1-alpha) theoretical coverage — set width = per-instance reliability proxy; (2) transitivity analysis detecting directed 3-cycles in preference graphs (A>B, B>C, C>A = nonsense). Applied on SummEval (news summarization) across four judges × four criteria (relevance, coherence, fluency, consistency), N=1,918 documents for correlation analysis. Methodology is transferable; primary evidence is non-clinical.
Eval question: How do you detect when an LLM judge is unreliable on a specific instance, not just in aggregate?
Methodology: Split conformal prediction across 1-5 Likert scores with theoretically-guaranteed (1-alpha) coverage. Set width = per-instance reliability proxy. Transitivity analysis: directed 3-cycle detection in preference graphs. Cross-judge correlation of set widths to test if judges agree on which instances are unreliable.
Measurement: Aggregate violation rate (3-cycles / total triples): 0.8-4.1%. Document-level violation rate (>=1 3-cycle per doc): 33-67%. Spearman correlation (set width vs reliability): +0.576 (N=1,918, p<10^-100). Cross-judge set-width correlation: r̄=0.32-0.38. Criterion set sizes: relevance ~3.0 (most reliable) → fluency/consistency ~4.9 (least).
Key findings: Aggregate consistency (0.8-4.1%) is deceptively high — per-document transitivity violation (33-67%) is the norm, not the exception. Prediction set width is a defensible per-instance reliability proxy (strong Spearman). Judges partially agree on which instances are difficult — enough signal to flag. Criterion-level reliability gradient matches the clinical objective-vs-subjective pattern independently reported.
Soft spots: arXiv preprint, under review, not peer-reviewed. Applied to SummEval (news) — not clinical. Single Likert range (1-5); does not test binary or ordinal tasks. Conformal coverage assumes exchangeability of calibration and test sets — may not hold under deployment drift. Methodology complexity limits easy deployment.
Strengths: Moves LLM-judge reliability analysis from aggregate to per-instance (rare and useful). Conformal prediction provides theoretical coverage guarantees. Transitivity violations are easy to explain (A>B>C>A = nonsense). Spearman correlation (+0.576, extreme p) is strong evidence the proxy tracks reliability. Criterion-level gradient aligns with independent clinical evidence (PQ-110) — converging support.
Relevance: Layer 1: Cross-task — LLM-as-judge reliability diagnostics. Layer 2: Methodological complement to KTP #8 — adds per-instance reliability checking to the existing 'validated but bounded' framing. Candidate Statistician_Guide.md technique once a clinical replication exists. Transitivity violations could be tested on any LLM-judge-scored kit output today.
Verdict reasoning: Methodologically rigorous, statistically sophisticated, addresses a real gap (per-instance vs aggregate judge reliability). But preprint, non-clinical corpus, no clinical replication. Transitivity finding (33-67% per-doc) is striking enough to be worth tracking; conformal-prediction machinery is defensible if someone ports it to a clinical benchmark. Verify: watch for peer-review acceptance and clinical replication in the next 3-6 months.
