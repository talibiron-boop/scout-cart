Han B, Barnes T, Reddy CD, Shin AY. Evaluating Large Language Model–Generated Clinical Summaries Through a Dual-Perspective Framework: Retrospective Observational Study. JMIR AI. 2026 Feb 10;5:e85221. doi:10.2196/85221.
https://ai.jmir.org/2026/1/e85221
Overview: Retrospective observational study evaluating GPT-4o mini summaries of 50 pediatric cardiac ICU progress notes. Assessment & plan sections deidentified, summarized with standardized prompts, evaluated separately by 8 pediatric cardiologists and 10 parents (2 of each per summary), each using their own 4-point Likert rubric. Headline finding: parents rated summaries more helpful than physicians did, with statistically significant disagreement on what counts as 'helpful.' Task type: Summarization. LLM: GPT-4o mini. Signal: Mixed. Setting: Pediatric cardiac ICU. Target: Patient/family-facing summary drafting. Volume: 50 notes. Clinicians: 8 cardiologists + 10 parents. Human review: Always.
Eval question: When a summary is intended to serve both clinicians and a lay audience, can a single rubric capture quality — or are dual rubrics required?
Methodology: Deidentification + standardized prompt for GPT-4o mini summarization; two evaluator pools (cardiologists, parents) with separate rubrics; 4-point Likert scales for helpfulness/readability/accuracy; Krippendorff's α for inter-rater reliability per pool; Mann-Whitney U test for parent-vs-physician helpfulness; Flesch-Kincaid grade level for readability.
Measurement: Helpfulness (parent): 3.25-3.36/4. Helpfulness (physician): 2.97/4. Clinical accuracy (physician): 3.19/4. Readability (parent): 3.36/4. Flesch-Kincaid grade level: 10.6 (target was 6-8). Inter-rater α: 0.69 (physicians), 0.75 (parents). Parent-vs-physician helpfulness: Mann-Whitney U=3897; z=2.69; P=.007.
Key findings: Parents rated summaries more helpful than physicians (P=.007). Readability target (grade 6-8) overshot by ~3 grades. Inter-rater reliability moderate (α=0.69 physicians, 0.75 parents). Implication: a 'helpful summary' by physician judgment is not the same as a 'helpful summary' by family judgment — single-rubric evaluation can miss this gap.
Soft spots: N=50 is small; pediatric cardiac ICU is a narrow specialty. Single LLM (GPT-4o mini), single prompt. 4-point Likert is coarse; α 0.69 (physicians) suggests headroom. 'Parents' is a proxy for 'lay reader' — generalization to adult patient populations untested. Mann-Whitney result hinges on a moderate effect size in a small sample.
Strengths: Direct measurement of parent-vs-physician disagreement on the same summaries. Reading-grade measurement quantifies a specific failure (target 6-8 → observed 10.6). Peer-reviewed (JMIR AI). Transparent rubric design. Methodologically simple — replicable for any patient/family-facing summarization kit.
Relevance: Layer 1: Summarization, generation aimed at lay audiences. Layer 2: Adjacent to Discharge Letter and Nuclear Medicine kits if there's a patient/family-facing variant. Reinforces Summarization §3 logic that completeness/accuracy ≠ helpfulness; argues for a separate lay-reader rubric when deployment target is patient-facing. Distinct from PQ-51-FU (different paper, simpler design, sharper empirical anchor).
Verdict reasoning: Methodologically clean and peer-reviewed, with concrete empirical anchor (P=.007 parent-vs-physician helpfulness disagreement; 10.6 grade level vs target 6-8). But user previously skipped dual-perspective work (PQ-51-FU 2026-03-19) and this paper's scope is narrow (pediatric cardiac ICU, N=50, single LLM). Hold at 'Interesting, verify' rather than Promote so user can decide whether dual-perspective methodology is back in scope. If promoted, refines Summarization §3 with a parent/lay-reader rubric pattern; if skipped, log the consistent dual-perspective skip pattern for future scout filtering.

Task: Classification/clinical reasoning MCQ. Signal: Explicit. Setting: Pediatric reasoning. Volume: 77 questions × 6 models × 2 modes.
Eval question: How sensitive is benchmark performance to input packaging — and what does that imply for benchmark interpretation?
Methodology: Same questions delivered simultaneously vs in sequential batches of 10; 6 commercial LLMs in parallel; accuracy per condition × model; variance attribution: delivery structure vs model identity.
Measurement: Accuracy per (model × delivery); range/IQR of accuracy across models within each delivery condition; statistical test for inter-model difference within batch (n.s.).
Key findings: Simultaneous delivery: 38-90% (range = 52pp). Batch delivery (10 at a time): 83-88% (range = 5pp, n.s.). Delivery structure DOMINATED model choice as source of variance. Implication: cross-benchmark model rankings may be artifacts of input packaging, not capability.

Relevance: Layer 1: Classification, extraction, any benchmark interpretation. Layer 2: Strengthens PQ-91 (non-determinism) and KTP #9 (input perturbation as adversarial axis). Vachhani B, Shrisvastava K, Nema P, Chiranthan S. Beyond Literal Summarization: Redefining Hallucination for Medical SOAP Note Evaluation. arXiv:2604.14829v1. 2026 Apr 16.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14829
Overview: Argues that lexical/literal faithfulness scoring misclassifies valid clinical reasoning — synonym mapping, finding abstraction, diagnostic inference, guideline-consistent planning — as 'hallucination.' Empirically: lexical evaluation produces 35% hallucination rate on SOAP notes; inference-aware rubric drops it to 9% — and the 9% are genuine safety concerns. Provides typology of 'valid abstraction vs unsupported claim' with worked examples. Task: Summarization (SOAP). Signal: Mixed. Setting: Outpatient documentation. Volume: Methodology paper + worked examples.
Eval question: What counts as a hallucination in clinical summarization — and how do you avoid penalizing valid reasoning as a 'fact'?
Methodology: Define inference-aware faithfulness: a claim is faithful if (a) literally supported OR (b) derivable via clinically valid inference from supported facts. Categorize 'valid abstraction' types: synonym, generalization (finding → category), diagnostic inference, guideline-consistent planning. Categorize 'genuine hallucination' types: unsupported invention, unsupported negation, plan beyond evidence. Apply both rubrics to same SOAP note set; compare rates.
Measurement: Hallucination rate per rubric (lexical vs inference-aware); per-claim Supported/Inference-Supported/Unsupported labels; agreement between independent reviewers.
Key findings: Lexical hallucination rate: 35%. Inference-aware hallucination rate: 9%. The 9% are genuine safety concerns (would harm if acted on). The 26pp gap dominated by valid synonym + abstraction patterns.
Soft spots: Preprint (not peer-reviewed). 'Inference-aware' requires clinical raters who can judge what counts as valid inference — harder to scale than lexical. Doesn't fully address omission (only commission) — should pair with completeness pass. 'Guideline-consistent' inference needs explicit guideline corpus.
Strengths: Directly addresses over-counting failure of standard hallucination rubrics. Immediately operational typology. Quantitative anchor (35% → 9%) makes the case empirically. Aligns with Nuclear Medicine kit's Supported/Not-Supported/Fabricated rubric (PQ-45 VeriFact, PQ-47 CREOLA).
Relevance: Layer 1: Summarization, generation. Layer 2: Directly applicable to Nuclear Medicine kit's per-statement rubric. Suggests refinement: split 'Supported' into 'Literally Supported' vs 'Inference-Supported' to avoid over-flagging valid abstraction. Updates Summarization §3 with calibration of what counts as hallucination.
Verdict reasoning: Important methodology paper that fixes real over-counting problem in standard hallucination rubric. 35%→9% gap is operationally significant: a kit using lexical rubrics will reject good summaries as unsafe, eroding clinician trust. Preprint status mild soft spot but framework immediately usable.

Eval question: Can a calibrated LLM jury substitute for a second human reviewer — and does isotonic calibration matter?
Methodology: Multi-model LLM jury scoring (multiple models per diagnosis, aggregated); two independent human expert panels score same diagnoses; isotonic regression calibrates jury distribution to one panel; held-out human re-score panel for external validation; ordinal agreement (Spearman, weighted κ) and concordance reported.
Measurement: Spearman correlation (target ≥ inter-panel correlation); weighted κ (target ≥ inter-panel κ); concordance with held-out panel; calibration improvement (delta vs uncalibrated).
Key findings: Calibrated jury achieves ordinal agreement comparable to inter-panel agreement. Uncalibrated jury under-performs panel agreement. Isotonic calibration accounts for most of the improvement. Real-world (middle-income) clinical scope — generalization signal beyond academic-medicine benchmarks.
Soft spots: Preprint, not yet peer-reviewed. Middle-income setting strength for generalization but unique scope. Calibration requires held-out human panel — circular requirement (if you have one, you may not need the jury). Multi-model jury cost not addressed.
