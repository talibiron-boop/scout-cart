Guo KH, Yan C, Baidya A, Brown K, Goa X, Xiong J, Yin Z, Malin BA. CLEAR: Revealing How Noise and Ambiguity Degrade Reliability in LLMs for Medicine. arXiv:2605.01011v2. 2026 May 1 (v1), 2026 May 9 (v2).
https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01011
Overview: Full-text deep-dive on the CLEAR paper after user 'more' decision on PQ-129. CLEAR (CLinical Evaluation of Ambiguity and Reliability) perturbs three established medical MCQ benchmarks — **MedQA, MedMCQA, JAMA Clinical Challenge** — along three input-side ambiguity axes: option count, abstention availability, semantic framing of the abstention option. Defines the **humility deficit** (correct-identification rate minus abstention rate when no correct answer is presented) as a model-scale-aware reliability metric. Total evaluation scale: **2.6 million inferences** = 1,200 queries/dataset (open-source models) or 400 (API models) × 3 datasets × multiple distractor counts (k ∈ {1,2,3} or {1,2,3,4}) × 2 prompting modes (base + CoT). No physician validation of either perturbations or outputs — perturbations are programmatic transforms of public benchmark items, evaluation is benchmark-automatic. No IRR/kappa reported.
Eval question: [same as PQ-129, plus the user's deep-dive questions] What models and benchmarks are tested? What are the actual humility-deficit numbers? Did physicians validate any of this? Is an operational threshold proposed? Are the three perturbation axes orthogonal or interacting?
Methodology: **Models (17 total):** Llama 3.2 (1B), Llama 3.1 (8B/70B), Qwen 2.5 (1.5B/3B/7B/72B), Qwen 3 (4B/8B/14B/32B), Gemma 3 (1B/4B/12B/27B), GPT-4o, GPT-5.2 Chat. **Benchmarks:** MedQA, MedMCQA, JAMA Clinical Challenge. **Three perturbation axes:** (1) increase number of plausible distractor options k∈{1,2,3} or {1,2,3,4}; (2) insert/omit an explicit abstention option; (3) reframe the abstention semantically as 'None of the above' (NA) vs 'I don't know' (IDK). **Two prompting modes:** base + chain-of-thought. **Sample:** 1,200 queries/dataset for open-source models, 400 for API models. **Total inferences:** ~2.6 M. **No physician validation** — perturbations are template-based programmatic transforms on existing benchmark items.
Measurement: Per-axis degradation curves on accuracy and abstention-rate. Humility deficit (scalar per model per condition) = correct-identification rate − correct-abstention rate when no correct answer is offered. Trend analysis across model scale (parameter count within family) and across families. No reliability statistics (no IRR/kappa) — automatic benchmark evaluation. No clinical-significance threshold for the humility deficit proposed.
Key findings: Verbatim numerical anchors from the published results: (a) **GPT-5.2 on JAMA Clinical Challenge abstains 'just 30% and 50% of the time' while incorrectly selecting an answer '70% and 50% of the time'** when no correct option is available — i.e., the frontier API model fails to abstain in roughly half of these no-correct-answer items. (b) **'Abstention rates decline nearly three to four times quicker than accuracy' for GPT-5.2 per additional distractor added** — abstention is more brittle than accuracy under information dilution. (c) **Semantic framing matters: GPT-5.2 abstains over 50% under NA ('None of the above') framing but only 25% under IDK ('I don't know') framing** in JAMA-CC — same model, same items, ~2× difference from a wording change. (d) Humility deficit worsens with model scale across families (visualized in Fig 3c; no per-model deficit table). (e) No interaction analysis between the three axes — only main effects.
Soft spots: (a) **No physician validation** of perturbations or outputs — the framework treats benchmark-automatic evaluation as ground-truth, which inherits whatever annotation noise MedQA/MedMCQA/JAMA-CC carry. (b) **No interaction analysis** — the three axes (option count × abstention × framing) are tested independently; we don't know whether degradation compounds, is additive, or is dominated by one axis. (c) **No operational threshold** for humility deficit — useful as a comparative diagnostic but no proposed cut-off for 'safe to deploy.' (d) **Per-model humility deficit values are not tabulated** — Fig 3c is visual only, so cross-model comparison requires reading data points off a chart. (e) **Older API model snapshot** — GPT-4o + GPT-5.2 Chat; no Claude / Gemini Pro / o-series models tested, leaving the cross-vendor comparison incomplete. (f) **Open-source model coverage skews small** (most ≤72B); large-scale frontier behavior beyond GPT-5.2 not characterized.
Strengths: (a) **Scale of evaluation** — 2.6 M inferences with 17 models × 3 benchmarks is the largest perturbation study of medical-LLM reliability published to date. (b) **Three concrete operational findings with verbatim numbers** (50/50 abstention failure, 3-4× abstention-vs-accuracy decline rate, 2× framing effect) are decision-relevant and falsifiable. (c) **Humility deficit construct** is methodologically clean — single scalar that captures a safety-relevant property current benchmarks miss. (d) **Open-source-heavy model panel** lets the scaling claim be tested within families (Qwen 2.5 1.5B→72B, Gemma 3 1B→27B), not just across families. (e) **Programmatic perturbations** mean the framework is independently reproducible without re-annotation.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Classification (MCQ structure) and Generation (input-side robustness). Layer 2 (kit connection): This deep-dive confirms PQ-129's promotion case but **constrains how to cite it.** Specifically: (i) cite the 50/50 GPT-5.2 abstention finding, the 3-4× decline-rate finding, and the NA-vs-IDK 2× framing finding as concrete numerical anchors for KTP #9 — these are the verbatim numbers, not the abstract gesture; (ii) when wiring into G-02 KTP #9 alongside PQ-125-FU CUE (benign elicitation stability) and PQ-77/PQ-59 (adversarial), make explicit that **humility deficit ≠ adversarial robustness ≠ benign elicitation stability** — three distinct input-side properties; (iii) flag the no-physician-validation soft spot so the user doesn't over-claim from a benchmark-automatic study.
Verdict reasoning: Promote (the deep-dive confirms the prior verdict; the numerical anchors now support a precise KTP #9 sub-bullet). The framework is a clean addition to the input-robustness vocabulary even with those gaps.

Jia Q, Wen Y, Liu Y, Zhao H, Yu Q, Long Y, Sun D, Yu Y. Implicit bias in safety-aligned large language models: A multi-faceted evaluation of clinical decision-making and health equity. PLoS One. 2026 May 19;21(5):e0348819. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0348819.
https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0348819
Overview: Cross-sectional study testing whether safety-aligned LLMs (trained to pass explicit social-bias tests) still carry latent **implicit** biases that translate into clinical decision-making. Constructs **24 medical bias datasets** across six high-stakes categories (gender, race, socioeconomic status, health conditions, religion, healthcare systems). Tests **10 LLMs**. Three measurement methods: LLM Word Association Test (analog of IAT), LLM Relative Decision Test, and Paired-Prompt Analysis. Reports that explicit-bias-passing models still show systematic implicit bias whose magnitude predicts downstream discriminatory clinical decisions (p < 0.001).
Eval question: Standard safety alignment closes the explicit-bias loophole. Does it close the **implicit-bias** loophole — and if not, does the residual implicit bias actually change clinical decisions, or does it sit harmlessly in associations? This is a methodological question (how to measure latent bias) and a downstream-validity question (does the measurement predict harm).
Methodology: Three measurement methods applied to all 10 models × 24 datasets × 6 categories: (1) **LLM Word Association Test** — pairs social-group tokens with attribute tokens, measures association strength (analog of human IAT); (2) **LLM Relative Decision Test** — presents the model with paired clinical scenarios differing only in a demographic identifier, measures decision divergence; (3) **Paired-Prompt Analysis** — direct probing under matched prompts. Per-category mean bias score reported on a 0-1 scale.
Measurement: Per-category implicit-bias mean (0-1 scale). Downstream-impact regression: implicit-bias score → discriminatory-choice rate in clinical decision tasks, with p-value. Per-model breakdown to show whether 'advanced reasoning capabilities' reduce bias.
Key findings: **All 10 models exhibited systematic implicit biases across all 6 categories.** Strongest biases: **Race (mean 0.61), Socioeconomic Status (mean 0.56)**. **Advanced reasoning capabilities did not significantly reduce bias magnitude** — scale/reasoning is not a fix. **Implicit-bias strength significantly predicted discriminatory decisions in downstream clinical tasks (p < 0.001)** — these latent associations translate into behavior, not just internal weights. Conclusion: 'Current safety alignment techniques fail to eliminate implicit biases in large language models within the medical domain.'
Soft spots: (a) **Cross-sectional/observational** — no intervention arm; can't tell whether targeted de-biasing would reduce the downstream impact. (b) **'10 models examined' but not named in abstract** — need full text to map to model versions. (c) **No clinical-outcome endpoint** — downstream effect is on simulated decisions, not real patient outcomes. (d) The 0-1 implicit-bias scale is a constructed metric; comparability to other LLM-bias studies depends on operational definitions in methods. (e) **Sample of clinical scenarios** — 24 datasets is many for an LLM-eval study but small for population-coverage claims; per-subgroup power may be limited within categories.
Strengths: (a) **Methodological triangulation** — three independent measurement methods (association, relative decision, paired prompt) addressing the same construct strengthens the validity claim. (b) **Downstream-link is the core contribution** — many bias studies stop at the metric; this one regresses metric → behavior and gets p < 0.001. (c) **'Advanced reasoning doesn't help' finding** is non-obvious and policy-relevant. (d) **PLoS One peer-reviewed venue.** (e) **6 categories × 24 datasets** is a substantial coverage for category-level claims about gender / race / SES / etc.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Conversational + Classification (decision support). Layer 2 (kit connection): No existing kit measures implicit bias yet. **G-04 (safety_vs_effectiveness.md)** is the natural home for a new sub-section on implicit-bias-as-safety-gate, separate from the patient-facing unsafe-rate floor that PQ-130 anchors. **KTP #9 ties:** complements PQ-77/PQ-59/PQ-130 by adding a *demographic* safety axis — current KTP #9 evidence is about adversarial hallucination and patient-facing safety floor, not equity. **For evaluation methodology specifically:** the LLM-IAT / Relative Decision Test pattern is a reusable probe design for any kit that involves demographics.
Verdict reasoning: Promote. The downstream regression (implicit-bias score predicts discriminatory clinical decisions at p<0.001) is exactly the kind of validity-linking evidence that makes a methodology citable in operational guidance. The 'reasoning doesn't fix bias' negative finding is policy-relevant. Methodological triangulation across three measurement modes addresses the usual single-metric criticism. Soft spots (cross-sectional design, simulated decisions only) are real but don't undermine the core methodological contribution. Recommend Promote with G-04 sub-section on implicit-bias measurement methodology.

Tao X, Dong X, Zhu Q, Zhou X. OEMA: ontology-enhanced multi-agent collaboration framework for zero-shot clinical named entity recognition. JAMIA Open. 2026 May 14;9(3):ooag049. doi:10.1093/jamiaopen/ooag049.
https://academic.oup.com/jamiaopen/article/9/3/ooag049/8203456
Overview: Methodology paper proposing **OEMA** — a three-agent zero-shot clinical NER framework. Three agents: a **self-annotator** (generates candidate examples), a **discriminator** (filters tokens against SNOMED CT for clinical relevance), and a **predictor** (uses entity-type descriptions for inference). Tested on three benchmarks (I2B2 2010, MTSamples, VAERS) across multiple backbone LLMs (GPT-3.5, GPT-4.1, Gemini-2.5-flash). Headline claim: zero-shot OEMA matches supervised BioClinicalBERT under relaxed-match and consistently beats other zero-shot baselines under exact-match.
Eval question: Can zero-shot clinical NER (no labeled training data) close the gap to supervised BERT-style models if the prompting is structured as multi-agent collaboration with explicit ontology grounding? Methodology contribution = a reusable pattern for clinical extraction tasks where labels are expensive.
Methodology: Three-agent zero-shot NER pipeline: (1) **self-annotator** autonomously generates candidate entity examples from each input; (2) **discriminator** filters at the token level against SNOMED CT ontology — drops candidates not represented in SNOMED CT or labeled with low clinical relevance; (3) **predictor** issues final inference using entity-type descriptions to enhance type-level consistency. Tested on **I2B2 2010, MTSamples, VAERS** with **GPT-3.5, GPT-4.1, Gemini-2.5-flash** as backbones. Evaluation: exact-match and relaxed-match F1, compared against zero-shot baselines and supervised BioClinicalBERT / CRF.
Measurement: F1 (exact-match) and F1 (relaxed-match) per benchmark per backbone. Comparison: vs zero-shot baselines (other prompting strategies), vs supervised BioClinicalBERT, vs CRF. No IRR/kappa (NER is token-level automatic comparison against gold-standard annotations).
Key findings: (a) Under **exact-match**, OEMA **'consistently outperforms existing zero-shot baselines'** across all three benchmarks and all three backbones (specific F1 numbers in full text, not in abstract). (b) Under **relaxed-match**, OEMA performs **'comparably to the supervised BioClinicalBERT model'** while **'significantly outperforming the traditional CRF method.'** (c) Ontology grounding via SNOMED CT is the load-bearing component — without the discriminator, zero-shot accuracy collapses (impact magnitude in full text). (d) Approach generalizes across three backbones, suggesting the framework, not the model choice, is what's doing the work.
Soft spots: (a) **Exact F1 numbers not in abstract** — full text required for the headline 'matches BioClinicalBERT' claim to be quantitatively verified. (b) **English-only benchmarks** (I2B2, MTSamples, VAERS); generalization to non-English clinical text untested. (c) **SNOMED CT dependence** — for tasks where the relevant ontology is partial or different (e.g., specialty-specific terminologies), the discriminator may filter too aggressively. (d) **No clinician judgment loop** — the 'clinical relevance' filter is automated against ontology presence, not against a clinician's read of relevance. (e) **Single research group** preprint-to-journal path — no independent reproduction yet.
Strengths: (a) **Three backbones tested** — the consistency of the result across GPT-3.5 / GPT-4.1 / Gemini-2.5-flash strengthens the methodology claim. (b) **Three diverse benchmarks** including the real-world I2B2 2010 — not benchmark-overfit. (c) **JAMIA Open peer-reviewed**. (d) **Zero-shot matching supervised BERT on relaxed-match** is a non-trivial result for clinical NLP — the supervised-models-only stance is being challenged. (e) **Multi-agent pattern is reusable** — even if the specific result doesn't transfer, the self-annotator + discriminator + predictor pattern with ontology grounding is a methodology template.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Extraction (the central task type in the Extraction §2 section of evaluation_methods_by_task.md). Layer 2 (kit connection): **VAP Extraction kit** could use the OEMA pattern as a candidate prompt-engineering reference if the user wants to compare per-stage agentic vs. single-prompt extraction. **Delirium Extraction kit** similarly. The methodology contribution is the **ontology-grounded discriminator** — for any clinical NER task where SNOMED CT (or another standardized ontology) covers the target entities, this is a structured way to reduce hallucinated entities without supervised data.
Verdict reasoning: Interesting, verify. The 'zero-shot matches supervised BioClinicalBERT under relaxed-match' headline is significant if verified, but the abstract doesn't give the F1 numbers — the magnitude is unverified until full text. Three backbones × three benchmarks is methodologically strong; the multi-agent + ontology-grounding pattern is reusable. The framework deserves a card and a watch-flag for full-text confirmation of the numerical gap-closing claim before promoting to Guidance.

https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.03904
Overview: Prompt-only intervention for confidence-aware abstention. **I-CALM** does three things in the prompt: (1) **elicits verbal confidence** from the model, (2) **announces an explicit reward scheme** that rewards abstention over wrong answers, (3) **incorporates normative principles** emphasizing truthfulness and humility. No model modification, no fine-tuning, no retrieval. Tested on **GPT-5 mini** using **PopQA** (factual QA — not clinical). Headline frame: 'an abstention-hallucination frontier' as a function of how strongly abstention is rewarded in the prompt.
Eval question: Can the abstention-vs-hallucination tradeoff be steered with prompt-only interventions (no fine-tuning), and if so, how do prompt design choices (confidence elicitation, reward-scheme announcement, normative principles) interact with hallucination rate on factual questions? This is the prompt-optimization side of the same question CLEAR (PQ-129) approaches from the input-perturbation side.
Methodology: Three prompt components combined: (i) instruction to verbalize a confidence score, (ii) explicit announcement that abstention is rewarded relative to wrong answers (reward scheme stated in the prompt), (iii) humility/truthfulness normative-principle insertion. Tested on **GPT-5 mini** with **PopQA** factual benchmark. Varies the abstention reward to trace out the 'abstention-hallucination frontier' — accuracy vs. abstention curve under different reward strengths.
Measurement: False-answer rate, abstention rate, and selective accuracy (accuracy among answered items). Frontier curve: false-answer rate as a function of abstention reward intensity. No clinical-significance threshold proposed.
Key findings: (a) Prompt-only interventions **'reduce false-answer rates by identifying error-prone cases and shifting them to abstention.'** (b) The framework **'improves selective answering on factual questions without retraining'** — i.e., a free intervention if it transfers. (c) Authors trace an **'abstention-hallucination frontier'** by varying abstention rewards. (d) **Effects vary across models and datasets** — explicit caveat that generalization is not assumed. (e) **No numerical anchors in the abstract** (no headline false-answer-rate-before-vs-after).
Soft spots: (a) **PopQA is not clinical** — entire result is on factual QA; clinical-domain transfer is asserted by analogy, not tested. (b) **Single tested model (GPT-5 mini)** — generalization to clinical-deployment-relevant frontier models (Claude 4.5, GPT-5 full, o-series) untested. (c) **Verbal confidence is known unreliable in healthcare contexts** — PQ-91 evidence flagged this for clinical tasks; I-CALM's reliance on self-reported confidence may not survive medical translation. (d) **No clinical/physician validation.** (e) **Preprint, single group, no peer review.** (f) Abstract reports qualitative direction-only; reproducibility of magnitude depends on full-text numbers.
Strengths: (a) **Prompt-only, zero-cost-to-deploy** intervention is a deployment-relevant lever. (b) **Explicit decomposition** of the intervention into three components (confidence, reward scheme, normative principles) lets future work ablate each. (c) **Frontier framing** (accuracy vs abstention as a continuous tradeoff) is methodologically right — this is how the tradeoff should be characterized rather than 'is abstention up or down?' (d) **Adjacent to CLEAR (PQ-129)** — the input-perturbation framework and the prompt-intervention framework attack the same abstention problem from two sides; useful as a complementary pair if both verify.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Generation / Conversational / Classification (any task with an abstention option). Layer 2 (kit connection): **For the optimize-skill knowledge base** specifically — `Prompt_Optimization_Guide.md` does not currently catalog a 'reward-scheme announcement' intervention as a distinct lever, and `prompt_interventions_by_task.md` does not list abstention-rewarding prompts as a documented pattern. I-CALM is the candidate template if (and only if) the clinical transfer is verified. For KTP #9: complements PQ-129 (CLEAR) and PQ-125-FU (CUE) — input-side perturbation + benign elicitation stability + prompt-only abstention intervention are three independent input-robustness levers.
Verdict reasoning: Interesting, verify. The methodology is clean and complements PQ-129 from the prompt side, but **the clinical-domain transfer is asserted, not tested** — PopQA is not clinical. Arora RK, Wei J, Hicks RS, Bowman P, Quiñonero-Candela J, Tsimpourlas F, Sharman M, Shah M, Vallone A, Beutel A, Heidecke J, Singhal K. HealthBench: Evaluating Large Language Models Towards Improved Human Health. arXiv:2505.08775v1. 2025 May 13. (OpenAI)
https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.08775
Overview: OpenAI's open-source benchmark for measuring LLM performance and safety in healthcare. **5,000 multi-turn conversations** between a model and either an individual user or a healthcare professional, authored by **262 physicians** practicing in **60 countries**, proficient in **49 languages**, trained in **26 medical specialties**. Each conversation carries its own physician-written rubric — **48,562 unique grading criteria total**. Grading is performed by an LLM judge (GPT-4.1) whose decisions were validated against physician judges before release. Released as code + data via the public OpenAI simple-evals repository. Three subsets distributed: full HealthBench, HealthBench Hard (challenging cases — top model scores ~32%), and HealthBench Consensus (criteria all physician annotators agreed on).
Eval question: How do you evaluate a healthcare LLM on real conversational behavior — not static MCQ benchmarks — across many specialties, languages, and clinical contexts, at a scale and grading consistency no manual physician panel could sustain? HealthBench's answer: scale rubric authoring across hundreds of physicians, scale grading via a validated LLM judge, and release the whole pipeline open-source.
Methodology: Conversation construction: 262 physicians authored 5,000 multi-turn dialogues covering 7 themes (emergency referrals, context-seeking, global health, health data tasks, expertise-tailored communication, responding under uncertainty, response depth) × 5 behavioral axes (accuracy, completeness, communication quality, context awareness, instruction following). Each conversation comes with a physician-authored rubric (mean ~10 criteria per conversation; 48,562 criteria total). Each criterion weighted by physician judgment of importance. Grading: GPT-4.1 grades model responses against the rubric; LLM-judge accuracy validated against held-out physician adjudication before publication. Subsets: HealthBench Hard is a curated low-headroom slice (top frontier score ~32%); HealthBench Consensus is the criteria with unanimous physician agreement.
Measurement: Per-conversation: weighted-rubric score (sum of satisfied-criterion weights / total weights). Per-model: mean score across the 5,000 conversations, plus per-theme and per-axis breakdowns. HealthBench Hard reports a separate headline number for the difficult slice. The LLM-judge-vs-physician-judge validation results are reported in the methods section as the load-bearing claim for trustworthiness of the automated grading.
Key findings: Headline scores (verbatim from paper): GPT-3.5 Turbo **16%**, GPT-4o **32%**, o3 **60%**. HealthBench Hard top score **32%** (substantial frontier headroom remains). **GPT-4.1 nano outperforms GPT-4o at 25× lower cost** — a methodologically important finding because it decouples 'newer/bigger model' from 'better HealthBench score' and supports nuanced model-selection decisions. The benchmark is sensitive enough to distinguish frontier-tier models from each other.
Soft spots: (a) **LLM-as-judge is the grader** — even though validated against physicians, the judge inherits whatever biases the GPT-4.1 model brings to medical content. The PMC follow-up critique flags this explicitly. (b) **Conversations are physician-authored, not transcribed from real practice** — selection effects in what physicians choose to write vs what real patients/clinicians actually ask. (c) **English-dominant rubrics in practice** even though 49 languages are represented — full per-language equity not quantified in the abstract. (d) **GPT-4.1 grading GPT-4.1-family responses** is a potential same-vendor confound that user studies need to consider for relative model comparisons. (e) **No clinical-outcome endpoint** — HealthBench measures response quality against rubric, not whether following the response leads to better care.
