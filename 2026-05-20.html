PQ-135 Promote
Arora RK, Wei J, Hicks RS, Bowman P, Quiñonero-Candela J, Tsimpourlas F, Sharman M, Shah M, Vallone A, Beutel A, Heidecke J, Singhal K. HealthBench: Evaluating Large Language Models Towards Improved Human Health. arXiv:2505.08775v1. 2025 May 13. (OpenAI)
https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.08775
Overview: OpenAI's open-source benchmark for measuring LLM performance and safety in healthcare. **5,000 multi-turn conversations** between a model and either an individual user or a healthcare professional, authored by **262 physicians** practicing in **60 countries**, proficient in **49 languages**, trained in **26 medical specialties**. Each conversation carries its own physician-written rubric — **48,562 unique grading criteria total**. Grading is performed by an LLM judge (GPT-4.1) whose decisions were validated against physician judges before release. Released as code + data via the public OpenAI simple-evals repository. Three subsets distributed: full HealthBench, HealthBench Hard (challenging cases — top model scores ~32%), and HealthBench Consensus (criteria all physician annotators agreed on).
Eval question: How do you evaluate a healthcare LLM on real conversational behavior — not static MCQ benchmarks — across many specialties, languages, and clinical contexts, at a scale and grading consistency no manual physician panel could sustain? HealthBench's answer: scale rubric authoring across hundreds of physicians, scale grading via a validated LLM judge, and release the whole pipeline open-source.
Methodology: Conversation construction: 262 physicians authored 5,000 multi-turn dialogues covering 7 themes (emergency referrals, context-seeking, global health, health data tasks, expertise-tailored communication, responding under uncertainty, response depth) × 5 behavioral axes (accuracy, completeness, communication quality, context awareness, instruction following). Each conversation comes with a physician-authored rubric (mean ~10 criteria per conversation; 48,562 criteria total). Each criterion weighted by physician judgment of importance. Grading: GPT-4.1 grades model responses against the rubric; LLM-judge accuracy validated against held-out physician adjudication before publication. Subsets: HealthBench Hard is a curated low-headroom slice (top frontier score ~32%); HealthBench Consensus is the criteria with unanimous physician agreement.
Measurement: Per-conversation: weighted-rubric score (sum of satisfied-criterion weights / total weights). Per-model: mean score across the 5,000 conversations, plus per-theme and per-axis breakdowns. HealthBench Hard reports a separate headline number for the difficult slice. The LLM-judge-vs-physician-judge validation results are reported in the methods section as the load-bearing claim for trustworthiness of the automated grading.
Key findings: Headline scores (verbatim from paper): GPT-3.5 Turbo **16%**, GPT-4o **32%**, o3 **60%**. HealthBench Hard top score **32%** (substantial frontier headroom remains). **GPT-4.1 nano outperforms GPT-4o at 25× lower cost** — a methodologically important finding because it decouples 'newer/bigger model' from 'better HealthBench score' and supports nuanced model-selection decisions. The benchmark is sensitive enough to distinguish frontier-tier models from each other.
Soft spots: (a) **LLM-as-judge is the grader** — even though validated against physicians, the judge inherits whatever biases the GPT-4.1 model brings to medical content. The PMC follow-up critique flags this explicitly. (b) **Conversations are physician-authored, not transcribed from real practice** — selection effects in what physicians choose to write vs what real patients/clinicians actually ask. (c) **English-dominant rubrics in practice** even though 49 languages are represented — full per-language equity not quantified in the abstract. (d) **GPT-4.1 grading GPT-4.1-family responses** is a potential same-vendor confound that user studies need to consider for relative model comparisons. (e) **No clinical-outcome endpoint** — HealthBench measures response quality against rubric, not whether following the response leads to better care. (f) The PMC follow-up summarizes the field's read: 'advancing AI evaluation in healthcare, but not yet clinically ready' — meaning HealthBench scores do not yet validate deployment in regulated clinical settings.
Strengths: (a) **Largest rubric-based clinical LLM benchmark in existence by an order of magnitude** — 48,562 criteria from 262 physicians is unmatched. (b) **Open-source data + code** via [openai/simple-evals](https://github.com/openai/simple-evals) — fully reproducible, citable, and re-runnable on any future model. (c) **LLM-judge-vs-physician validation built in** — the rubric-based-with-LLM-grader pattern is now an empirically defensible methodology, not just an assumption. (d) **Multi-turn conversational format** more deployment-realistic than single-turn MCQ benchmarks. (e) **Hard + Consensus subsets** support both headroom-discovery and high-confidence-criterion analysis. (f) **Cross-vendor frontier comparison** across the GPT family enables relative-improvement claims.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Conversational (primary), with sub-applications to Classification, Generation, Summarization where rubric criteria probe those behaviors. Layer 2 (kit connection): Not directly tied to any existing kit (no consumer/conversational kit yet), **but methodologically reinforces three Guidance positions**: (i) **KTP #7 (benchmark-reality gap)** — HealthBench is the best-case rubric ceiling against which deployment-realistic studies (Brodeur PQ-124 real-ED, AgentClinic when imported) measure their gap; (ii) **KTP #8 (LLM-as-judge validated but bounded)** — HealthBench is now the canonical *large-scale* worked example of the validated-LLM-judge pattern, replacing smaller examples (PQ-54, PQ-41, S-10) as the headline reference; (iii) **Generation §4 (rubric-based grading)** — HealthBench's 48,562-criterion design is the largest physician-rubric benchmark and the strongest existence proof that rubric-with-criterion-weights scales as a methodology.
Verdict reasoning: Promote. Foundational benchmark for the conversational-LLM-in-healthcare space, openly available, methodologically substantial. Three Guidance cross-references identified (KTP #7, KTP #8, Generation §4). Honest caveat to record in the card: PMC follow-up critique flags 'not yet clinically ready' — HealthBench informs model selection and improvement, but does not substitute for deployment-stage evaluation.
PQ-135-FU Promote
Ravichandran S, Kumar S, Corga Da Silva R, Romano M, Berkels R, van der Heijden M, Fail O, Gnanapragasam VE. OpenAI's HealthBench in Action: Evaluating an LLM-Based Medical Assistant on Realistic Clinical Queries. arXiv:2509.02594v2. 2025 Aug 29 (v1), 2026 Feb 17 (v2 — updated DR. INFO scores + GPT-5 family comparisons).
https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.02594
Overview: **External application of HealthBench by a different team** (not an author follow-up) — evaluates **DR. INFO**, an agentic RAG-based clinical assistant, on the HealthBench Hard subset and reports comparative scores against the GPT-5 frontier family. v1 (Aug 2025) used pre-GPT-5 baselines; the **v2 revision (Feb 17 2026)** updates DR. INFO to a newer release and adds GPT-5 / GPT-5.1 / GPT-5.2 comparators. Authors frame the work as a case study in 'behavior-level, rubric-based evaluation for building reliable and trustworthy AI-enabled clinical support systems' — i.e., HealthBench as a deployment-evaluation tool, not just a model-comparison benchmark.
Eval question: Does HealthBench (specifically HealthBench Hard) work as an external deployment-evaluation instrument for a third-party agentic clinical assistant — and does an agentic RAG architecture meaningfully outperform unaugmented frontier LLMs on the harder rubric criteria?
Methodology: DR. INFO (agentic RAG-based clinical assistant) evaluated on HealthBench Hard alongside GPT-5 family models as comparators. Same rubric criteria, same LLM-judge grader as the main HealthBench. Comparison frame is direct numeric scores on HealthBench Hard. Methodology contribution: external use of the public HealthBench pipeline to evaluate a deployed-style system, demonstrating the benchmark works as an industry-facing yardstick — not just an OpenAI-internal model-comparison tool.
Measurement: HealthBench Hard score (rubric-weighted, LLM-judge-graded) per system. Comparison against published GPT-5 family numbers on the same subset.
Key findings: **DR. INFO scores 0.68 on HealthBench Hard**, compared with reported **GPT-5: 0.46, GPT-5.2: 0.42, GPT-5.1: 0.40**. Against similar systems (industry RAG-clinical-assistant peers), DR. INFO maintains a lead at **0.72**. The headline implication: agentic RAG with clinical retrieval can substantially exceed unaugmented frontier LLM HealthBench scores, but the comparison is between **architectures** (RAG-augmented agent vs raw LLM), not between **models in isolation** — so the result is about deployment architecture, not LLM weights.
Soft spots: (a) **Single-vendor self-evaluation** — DR. INFO's team is reporting on their own product against external baselines. Independent verification absent. (b) **Architecture-vs-model confound** — DR. INFO's 0.68 vs GPT-5's 0.46 spread is not directly attributable to the model alone; the RAG layer, retrieval corpus, and orchestration are all part of the score. (c) **No clinical-outcome arm** — same limitation as parent HealthBench. (d) **v2 revision history** — the published numbers shifted between v1 (Aug 2025) and v2 (Feb 2026), suggesting evaluation pipelines are still moving targets even for this benchmark. (e) **Comparator numbers for GPT-5.x may not be from author-replicated runs** — could be quoted from OpenAI's published values; methodology section needs full-text verification to confirm same-pipeline runs.
Strengths: (a) **First public worked example of an external team using HealthBench as a deployment-evaluation instrument** — establishes that the benchmark works as intended outside OpenAI. (b) **Direct architecture-vs-model headline number** — gives the user a concrete data point for the 'is retrieval worth it?' decision when designing a clinical-assistant kit. (c) **Up-to-date comparator panel** — v2's GPT-5 family numbers are the most current frontier baseline anyone has cited on HealthBench Hard. (d) **Methodology mirrors the user's own future deployment patterns** (agentic, retrieval-augmented, multi-turn clinical) — relevant precedent.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Conversational + Generation (clinical assistant) + Agentic. Layer 2 (kit connection): No existing kit yet maps to agentic clinical assistants, but **this is the closest published precedent for what a deployed-LLM evaluation framework looks like in 2026.** Methodologically reinforces KTP #7 (benchmark-reality gap is partially closed by HealthBench Hard, but rubric≠outcome remains), and provides the v2 GPT-5 family numbers as the current frontier baseline anchor.
Verdict reasoning: Promote. Adds the current (Feb 2026) GPT-5-family frontier scores on HealthBench Hard plus the first published external use of HealthBench as a deployment-evaluation instrument. Worth importing alongside the parent PQ-135 because the application paper is what makes HealthBench *cite-able for kit-design decisions* rather than just *citable for benchmark-comparison decisions*.
PQ-136 Promote
OpenAI. HealthBench Professional: A Benchmark of Realistic Tasks Encountered by Healthcare Workers. OpenAI, 2025. https://cdn.openai.com/dd128428-0184-4e25-b155-3a7686c7d744/HealthBench-Professional.pdf
https://cdn.openai.com/dd128428-0184-4e25-b155-3a7686c7d744/HealthBench-Professional.pdf
Overview: **Physician-facing companion to HealthBench.** Where the main HealthBench focuses on consumer / user-facing conversations, HealthBench Professional contains **525 physician-authored tasks across 3 clinician-facing use-case categories — care consult, writing and documentation, and medical research**. Each task is a single-turn or multi-turn dialogue between a clinician and an LLM, graded against example-specific physician-authored rubric criteria with importance-weighted criterion scoring. Design framing: 'meaningful' (improvements reflect better real-clinician task performance), 'trustworthy' (scores reflect true performance), 'challenging' (substantial headroom remains for current frontier models).
Eval question: How do you evaluate a healthcare LLM specifically on the tasks clinicians actually do — drafting documentation, supporting a consult, doing literature/research lookups — at sufficient methodological rigor to inform clinician-workflow product decisions? HealthBench Professional's answer: 525 physician-authored tasks across the three clinician-facing categories, same rubric methodology as the main benchmark.
Methodology: 525 tasks authored by physicians covering: (i) **care consult** — clinician seeking input on patient cases, (ii) **writing and documentation** — drafting notes, letters, summaries, (iii) **medical research** — literature/evidence lookup. Each task is a single-turn or multi-turn conversation with the model. Grading: example-specific rubric criteria, weighted by importance per physician judgment, evaluated via LLM-judge (same pipeline as main HealthBench).
Measurement: Per-task weighted-rubric score. Per-category aggregates (consult / documentation / research). Headline: frontier-model performance + remaining headroom; the report is framed around the gap that still exists for clinician-facing tasks.
Key findings: Three use-case categories operationally separated for the first time in a public benchmark. Substantial headroom across all three (specific numbers in the linked PDF — extraction blocked by binary-compressed format on auto-fetch; full read required before final card commit). The dataset is positioned explicitly as a **physician-workflow** benchmark, not a consumer-medical-question benchmark — addresses the gap that the main HealthBench's user-facing slant left.
Soft spots: (a) **OpenAI self-published** — same vendor that authors the grader (GPT-4.1) and many of the evaluated models. Independent third-party scoring not yet established. (b) **Smaller corpus (525 tasks vs 5,000)** means less per-category statistical power; per-subgroup confidence intervals matter and aren't in the abstract-level summary. (c) **Headline numbers and exact per-model scores not extracted in this card** — PDF binary-compressed; full-text confirmation needed before promoting the specific numbers to Guidance. (d) **Same LLM-judge-grading concerns** as main HealthBench (PQ-135 soft spot a). (e) **'Realistic' construction is still physician-authored, not transcribed from real workflows** — selection bias possible.
Strengths: (a) **First public benchmark of clinician-facing tasks** at this scale and with this methodology — fills the gap that the main HealthBench (consumer-leaning) left. (b) **Three clearly delineated use-case categories** map cleanly onto the kinds of LLM products you'd design for clinicians (consult-support, documentation, research). (c) **Same rubric + LLM-judge pipeline** as the main HealthBench — methodologically continuous, allows cross-benchmark comparison. (d) **Importance-weighted criteria** — not all rubric points are equal, design reflects clinical judgment about what matters. (e) **Open distribution** — publicly available PDF + likely simple-evals repo integration.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Generation (documentation), Classification + Generation (consult support), Summarization + Generation (medical research). Layer 2 (kit connection): **Directly relevant to clinician-facing kits.** Specifically: (i) Nuclear Medicine Summarization, Discharge Letter Summarization — both touch the 'writing and documentation' category; HealthBench Professional gives an external sanity-check benchmark for that category. (ii) Consultation Quality kit — directly maps to the 'care consult' category. (iii) Any future clinician-research-assistant kit — maps to the 'medical research' category. **For Guidance:** reinforces KTP #7 and Generation §4 alongside PQ-135, but with the clinician-workflow lens specifically.
Verdict reasoning: Promote. Clinician-workflow companion to HealthBench fills a real gap for kit-design reasoning. Soft-spot to record: the exact per-category numbers were not extractable from auto-fetch — the card should be flagged as 'numbers pending full-text confirmation' until the user reads the PDF or I extract via an alternative path. The methodology is solid and the categorical structure (consult / documentation / research) is decision-relevant for your kit portfolio.
