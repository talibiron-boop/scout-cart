Bassett BA, Rouillard A, Mundia S, Gramanie MC, Camara L, Dangor Z, Madhi SA, Morar K, Ncube MT, Kalla I, Saloojee H. Evaluating Multimodal LLMs for Inpatient Diagnosis: Real-World Performance, Safety, and Cost Across Ten Frontier Models. arXiv:2604.16980v1. 2026 Apr 18.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.16980
Overview: Real-world prospective evaluation of 10 frontier multimodal LLMs (incl. GPT-5.1, Gemini variants) on 539 inpatient cases at a South African tertiary public hospital. Inputs were the full admission packet — imaging, labs, notes, vitals — not text-only QA. Critically, the analysis prices each model and reports a Pareto-style cost-performance frontier. The LMIC framing differentiates it from the predominantly North-American/high-income KB literature. Task: multimodal diagnosis. LLMs: 10 frontier models. Setting: South African tertiary hospital. Volume: 539 inpatient cases. Comparator: routine ward diagnoses + expert panel.
Eval question: How should multimodal LLM evaluation in healthcare account for deployment cost as a co-equal axis with accuracy and safety, and does the cost-performance frontier change which model a hospital should pick — especially in resource-constrained settings?
Methodology: Prospective real-world inpatient cohort; 10 multimodal LLMs head-to-head on the same case material; multimodal inputs (imaging + labs + notes + vitals); diagnostic accuracy + expert-panel safety scoring; routine ward diagnoses as a real-world clinical comparator; per-model cost folded into a cost/performance Pareto comparison.
Measurement: Composite diagnostic accuracy score; safety score against expert panel; cross-model performance variation: <15%; cost-per-case (USD) plotted against composite score; comparison to routine ward diagnoses (significant outperformance on average).
Key findings: Performance was tightly clustered (<15% variation) across all 10 frontier models despite cost differences exceeding an order of magnitude. All models significantly outperformed routine ward diagnoses on average diagnostic and safety scores. GPT-5.1 ranked top on combined accuracy + safety. Several lower-cost models came within the 15% band of GPT-5.1 — in resource-constrained settings, affordability + robustness can dominate marginal accuracy gains. The Pareto frontier suggests the 'best clinical buy' is not the most expensive model.
Soft spots: Preprint, not yet peer-reviewed. Single-site (one South African tertiary hospital) — generalization to other LMICs (let alone HICs) unproven. Multimodal data preparation pipeline not fully detailed in abstract. Comparator quality (trainee vs senior ward calls) needs verification before the 'outperforms ward diagnoses' framing is taken at face value. 10-model snapshot — Pareto positions shift with each release. No subgroup analysis on case complexity / specialty surfaced. Cost framing assumes API pricing; doesn't include integration / safety-monitoring cost.
Strengths: Real prospective inpatient data — not benchmark proxies. LMIC setting is rare in our KB and methodologically important. Cost as a first-class evaluation axis is novel in clinical LLM evaluation. Multimodal real-world inputs (imaging + labs + notes + vitals) closer to deployment reality than text-only QA. 10-model head-to-head minimizes cherry-picking. Named clinical authors (Madhi, Saloojee — established academic clinicians) add credibility.
Relevance: Layer 1: Diagnosis, multimodal evaluation, cost-effectiveness, deployment evaluation, LMIC context. Layer 2: No direct existing-kit overlap (current kits are extraction/summarization/alerting), but the cost-performance methodology and LMIC context-dependency signal would belong in context_dependency.md and shape the Methodology table when a multimodal diagnostic kit is built. Useful background for any future kit considering 'which model do we pick' — challenges the 'always use the most capable' default.
Verdict reasoning: This is the kind of study the KB has been thin on — real prospective patient data outside North America/Europe, with cost surfaced as a first-class evaluation axis. The single-site preprint status is a real soft spot, but the methodology (10-model head-to-head on real cases with explicit cost analysis) is sound and the LMIC framing addresses a genuine equity-and-access gap. The headline finding (<15% variation across 10× cost) is operationally consequential — it changes the model-selection conversation. Worth promoting now and re-checking on peer review.

Mahbub M, Dams GM, Arnold J, Rizy C, Srinivasan S, Fielstein EM, Aghevli MA, Craig KL, Oliva EM, Erdos J, Trafton J, Danciu I. A multi-stage validation framework for trustworthy large-scale clinical information extraction using large language models. arXiv:2604.06028. 2026 Apr.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06028
Overview: Six-stage validation pipeline for LLM clinical IE at massive scale — 919,783 VA notes, substance use disorder diagnoses across 11 categories. Stages: prompt calibration → rule-based plausibility → semantic grounding → independent higher-capacity judge LLM → selective expert review → external predictive validity. Demonstrates trustworthy extraction at a scale where full expert review is infeasible. Task: extraction. Setting: VA. Volume: 919,783 clinical notes.
Eval question: How is LLM extraction validated when volume makes full expert review infeasible? Can multi-stage automated validation achieve trustworthy results at scale?
Methodology: Six stages: prompt calibration → rule plausibility filtering → semantic grounding → judge LLM → expert sample → external predictive validity (extraction quality measured by downstream clinical outcome — SUD specialty care engagement).
Measurement: Rule + semantic filtering removed 14.59% unreliable extractions. Judge LLM vs expert: Gwet's AC1 = 0.80. Primary LLM F1 = 0.80 (relaxed). External predictive validity AUC = 0.80 for SUD specialty care engagement (outperforms structured EHR codes alone).
Key findings: Multi-stage filtering catches ~15% of unreliable extractions before any human review; judge LLM achieves substantial agreement with experts (AC1=0.80) as a scalable QC gate; LLM-extracted diagnoses MORE predictive of actual care than structured EHR codes; per-stage decomposition enables diagnostic troubleshooting; pipeline enables trustworthy extraction at a scale where full expert annotation is impossible.
Soft spots: Preprint; single domain (SUD); VA-specific environment; 'relaxed matching' for F1 (strict would be lower); Gwet's AC1 inflated by high-agreement asymmetry (correct choice but limits comparability across studies); judge LLM has its own error patterns (not perfect ground truth).
Strengths: Quantified per-stage contribution; massive real-world scale; novel external predictive validity step (extraction quality measured by downstream clinical utility, not just agreement); statistically sophisticated (Gwet's AC1 used appropriately for high-agreement scenarios); each stage independently analyzable; real VA data, not synthetic.
Relevance: Layer 1: Extraction — multi-stage validation pipeline at scale. Layer 2: Direct relevance to VAP Extraction kit — staged validation mirrors our sanity check → retrospective pipeline at much larger scale. Predictive validity could inform production monitoring design (does extraction quality predict clinical outcomes?). Gwet's AC1 usage aligns with rare_events_evaluation.md.
Verdict reasoning: Addresses a genuine methodology gap — validation at scale where full expert annotation is infeasible. Practical 6-stage pipeline with quantified per-stage contribution. The external predictive validity step is novel and principled. Preprint limitation offset by massive scale, real data, clear methodology, and appropriate metric choices.

Hussain K, Malin BA, Yin Z, Rose SL, Kantarcioglu M. Blending human and LLM expertise to detect hallucinations and omissions in mental health chatbot responses. arXiv:2604.06216. 2026 Mar.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06216
Overview: Demonstrates that leading LLM judges achieve only ~52% accuracy for hallucination detection in mental health counseling data — near random. Proposes a hybrid: human domain expertise drives 5-dimension feature extraction (logical consistency, entity verification, factual accuracy, linguistic uncertainty, professional appropriateness) which feeds traditional ML classifiers. Hybrid F1: 0.717 (custom dataset), 0.849 (public benchmark). Omission detection harder (0.59-0.64 F1). Task: classification (hallucination + omission). Setting: mental health counseling chatbots.
Eval question: How reliable are pure LLM-as-judge approaches for hallucination detection in clinical conversational AI, and can human+LLM hybrid methods close the gap?
Methodology: Baseline test of leading LLM judges on mental health counseling data; hybrid approach with 5-dimension feature extraction (clinically grounded); traditional ML classifiers trained on those features; evaluated on custom annotated dataset + public benchmark; addresses both hallucination and omission detection.
Measurement: LLM-as-judge baseline ~52% accuracy (near random); hybrid F1 0.717 (custom) and 0.849 (public benchmark); omission detection 0.59-0.64 F1 across all methods.
Key findings: Pure LLM judges fail dramatically on mental-health-counseling data (~52%); failure is domain-specific — judges trained on factual domains miss nuanced therapeutic communication; hybrid substantially outperforms pure LLM-as-judge; omission detection harder than hallucination across all methods; interpretable features enable diagnostic analysis of failure modes.
Soft spots: Preprint; mental-health-chatbot is a specialized subdomain — 52% failure may not generalize; custom dataset details not fully available from abstract; hybrid feature engineering requires domain expertise (may not scale); traditional ML on extracted features less flexible than end-to-end approaches.
Strengths: Quantifies a critical LLM-as-judge limitation (52% on mental health) most studies miss because they focus on factual tasks; 5-dimension feature framework is interpretable and clinically grounded; addresses both hallucination AND omission; directly challenges the assumption that LLM-as-judge generalizes across clinical domains.
Relevance: Layer 1: Cross-task — LLM-as-judge limitations, hallucination + omission methodology. Layer 2: Extends KTP #8 — LLM-as-judge fails in domains needing nuanced clinical judgment (mental health counseling). Reinforces 'objective criteria good, subjective criteria poor' pattern. Omission > hallucination difficulty aligns with PQ-47 (CREOLA omission rates 3.45% > hallucination 1.47%).
Verdict reasoning: 52% LLM-as-judge failure is an important data point extending KTP #8. Mental-health-chatbot is a specialized subdomain, paper is preprint, and applicability to current core kits (extraction, summarization) is indirect. Hybrid methodology requires domain-specific feature engineering. Worth tracking as evidence for LLM-as-judge boundaries; not immediately actionable for the current kit portfolio.
