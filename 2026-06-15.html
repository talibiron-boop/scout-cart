PQ-141 Promote
Shyr C, Ren B, Hsu C-Y, Tinker RJ, Cassini TA, Hamid R, Wright A, Bastarache L, Peterson JF, Malin BA, Xu H. A statistical framework for evaluating repeatability and reproducibility of large language models in diagnostic reasoning. medRxiv (preprint). 2025 Nov 4. doi:10.1101/2025.08.06.25333170
https://www.medrxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.08.06.25333170
Overview: Methods paper giving clinical evaluators a way to QUANTIFY LLM output consistency separately from accuracy, using FDA-draft-guidance vocabulary. Distinguishes repeatability (same prompt, repeated runs) from reproducibility (across prompts), and within each, semantic (clinical meaning) from internal (token-level) variability. Models: ChatGPT-4, ChatGPT-4o-mini, Llama-3.2-1B. Datasets: 518 MedQA (USMLE) + 90 Undiagnosed Diseases Network rare-disease cases. 912,000 generations (R=100 runs per prompt-case-model). Vanderbilt informatics group.
Eval question: Consistency ('same input → same output') is usually assumed, not measured. How do you measure whether an LLM is consistent — and is consistency the same as being correct? (Answer: no.)
Methodology: Four 0–1 metrics: Semantic Repeatability (mean pairwise cosine similarity of MedEmbed-Large-v1 embeddings across runs); Internal Repeatability (mean across-run entropy, rescaled); Semantic Reproducibility (cosine similarity of prompt-specific mean embeddings across 5 prompts); Internal Reproducibility (mean abs diff in mean entropy across prompt pairs). 5 diagnostic-reasoning CoT prompts (Traditional/Differential/Intuitive/Analytic/Bayesian). R=100 runs; temp 0.5, top-k 30.
Measurement: Consistency reported 0–1 per metric; no fixed pass/fail threshold (the contribution is the measurement framework). Pairs with the existing Extraction §2 'report run-to-run variability with R repeats' guidance (PQ-91).
Key findings: Repeatability and reproducibility did NOT correlate with diagnostic accuracy — a model can be right without being consistent. Bayesian-reasoning CoT had significantly higher semantic repeatability. UDN rare-disease cases varied less across prompts than USMLE. Only for intuitive CoT was higher internal repeatability associated with accuracy (p<0.001); no association for the other four prompts.
Soft spots: Preprint; 3 models only; single temperature (0.5), so 'repeatability' blends decoding stochasticity with serving-stack non-determinism (the batching cause PB-07 isolates); embedding-based semantic similarity inherits the embedding model's blind spots; diagnostic-reasoning QA, not extraction/classification — porting needs adaptation.
Strengths: First clinical, large-N operationalization of the FDA repeatability/reproducibility vocabulary for LLMs; the accuracy≠consistency dissociation is clean and decision-relevant; separates semantic from internal variability rather than a single 'stability' number.
Relevance: Cross-cutting; most directly Extraction §2 (Non-Determinism as Evaluation Metric) and any generation/diagnostic-reasoning kit. The clinical statistical complement to PB-07 (PB-07 explains WHY numerical non-determinism exists; this gives clinicians HOW to measure it and shows it is orthogonal to accuracy). Anchors the R-Selector / Two-Determinisms planner drafts. If promoted: candidate note in Statistician_Guide.md (R repeats, semantic vs internal, FDA definitions) + Extraction §2 (operationalizes 'measure with R').
Verdict reasoning: This is the source scan #29 flagged as the verify-next-cycle lead (then 403'd), now reachable and fully verified. It closes the determinism thread: PB-07 (cause) + PQ-91 (accuracy-determinism tradeoff) + PQ-141 (clinical measurement framework, accuracy≠consistency). Preprint → cite as methodology, not as a fixed-threshold source.
PQ-142 Promote
Ye J(T), Tawfik D, Goodell AJ, Kotha NV, Buyyounouski MK, Bayati M. Scalable Stewardship of an LLM-Assisted Clinical Benchmark with Physician Oversight. arXiv (preprint). 2026 Apr 13. arXiv:2512.19691v3.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.19691
Overview: Methods paper on a validity problem the field mostly ignores: when a clinical benchmark's labels are LLM-assisted, how many are wrong and how do you fix them without re-labeling everything by hand? Proposes a three-phase stewardship protocol and shows corrected labels change both model rankings and downstream training. Stanford (Bayati group). 1,047 test instances; 50 physician-adjudicated for ground truth.
Eval question: If the reference standard is partly machine-generated, the leaderboard may be measuring label noise. How do you audit and correct an LLM-assisted benchmark at scale while spending scarce physician time only where it matters?
Methodology: Phase 1 — automated audit: tool-augmented LLM verifiers check labels against derivation metadata, flag suspect cases by supermajority across 5 runs. Phase 2 — independent recomputation: separate LLM agents recompute labels from the patient narrative alone (blinded), flag disagreements beyond ±5%. Phase 3 — physician adjudication of the highest-disagreement instances → ground truth.
Measurement: Label agreement vs physician ground truth (95% CIs); downstream model accuracy on physician-labeled tests; mislabel/incomputable rate.
Key findings: ≥27.3% of 1,047 instances likely mislabeled or incomputable. Recomputed labels: 74% physician agreement (95% CI 60–84%) vs 20% (11–33%) for originals. RL-trained models on corrected labels beat originals by +13.5pp (95% CI 10.6–16.6%). Frontier models re-scored on corrected labels: 16–23pp higher than originally reported.
Soft spots: Single benchmark; preprint. The audit/recompute layers are themselves LLM-based — circularity mitigated by physician adjudication on the residual, but non-adjudicated corrections rest on LLM consensus. ±5% tolerance and supermajority-of-5 are design choices, not derived.
Strengths: Quantifies a usually-invisible failure mode (benchmark label noise) and shows it flips conclusions; spends physician time only on the highest-disagreement residual (scalable); reports CIs and a downstream-utility test, not just agreement.
Relevance: Classification / Extraction / any benchmark-based evaluation. Strengthens KTP #7 (benchmark scores ≠ clinical performance) and KTP #8 (LLM-as-judge validated but bounded) with a concrete label-quality mechanism; methodologically adjacent to PQ-93 (Mahbub multi-stage validation) and PQ-140 (human + LLM-judge co-validation). If promoted: a note that LLM-assisted reference labels need an audit-and-adjudicate pass before they gate a decision.
Verdict reasoning: Clean, quantified contribution on benchmark label validity — directly relevant to anyone trusting an LLM-assisted reference standard. Preprint → cite the protocol and the 27.3% / 74%-vs-20% numbers with the single-benchmark caveat.
PQ-143 Interesting, verify
He Q, Bi D, Lu J, Yang M, Chen Z, et al. MLB: A Scenario-Driven Benchmark for Evaluating Large Language Models in Clinical Applications. arXiv (preprint). 2026 Jan 8. arXiv:2601.06193.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.06193
Overview: A large clinical benchmark organized by usage SCENARIO (medical reasoning vs patient-facing service vs safety), scored by an expert-SFT-trained judge model rather than string metrics. 22 datasets (17 new), 64 specialties, 300 licensed-physician annotators; judge trained on 19,000 expert-annotated samples. 10 models evaluated (largely non-US: Kimi-K2-Instruct, Baichuan-M2-32B).
Eval question: Can a judge model fine-tuned on expert annotations give reproducible, expert-aligned scoring across heterogeneous clinical scenarios — and does aggregate accuracy hide scenario-specific weakness?
Methodology: Scenario-partitioned datasets; judge model via SFT on 19K expert annotations, validated against held-out human judgment; 10 models scored with per-scenario disaggregation.
Measurement: Judge–human consistency: accuracy 92.1%, F1 94.37%, Cohen's κ 81.3%. Per-scenario accuracy plus a safety score.
Key findings: Top model (Kimi-K2-Instruct) 77.3% overall but 61.3% in patient-facing vs 87.8% in medical-reasoning — large scenario gap. A smaller model (Baichuan-M2-32B) scored highest on safety (90.6%) — targeted training beats scale for safety.
Soft spots: Preprint; mostly non-US models, so phrasing/guideline transfer to a US/Hebrew clinical setting is unclear; judge-validation is in-distribution (same annotation style for train and test); the 'scenario' taxonomy is bespoke, reusability unproven.
Strengths: Per-scenario disaggregation surfaces the patient-facing drop (reinforces PQ-102 task-vs-patient gap and PQ-101 patient-facing weakness); expert-SFT judge with reported agreement is a reusable pattern where a fixed rubric judge is too rigid.
Relevance: Classification / Generation / conversational; LLM-as-judge. Reinforces (does not introduce) the scenario/patient-facing-gap theme (PQ-102, PQ-101) and the validated-judge theme (KTP #8, PQ-135). Adds an expert-SFT-judge variant to the LLM-as-judge toolkit.
Verdict reasoning: Solid annotation scale and a useful judge-training pattern, but it mainly reinforces existing KB positions and uses non-US models on a bespoke taxonomy. Verify-grade rather than Promote; worth the user's eyes for the expert-SFT-judge idea.
