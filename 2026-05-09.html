Li AJ. Green Shielding: A User-Centric Approach Towards Trustworthy AI — LLM-Assisted Medical Diagnosis as a Case Study. arXiv:2604.24700. 2026 Apr 27.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24700
Overview: Follow-up deep-dive after user 'more' decision on PQ-125. Single-author arXiv preprint (Apr 27 2026) operationalizing the **CUE** criteria (Context / Utility / Elicitation) through a new 2,697-query **HCM-Dx** benchmark filtered from the first 20K examples of HealthCareMagic-100K. Three-tiered reference-diagnosis structure: plausible set (≤10 dx), highly likely set (1–3 dx), safety-critical set (1–3 dx). Released under CC BY 4.0 on HuggingFace as `aaronjli/HCM-Dx-3K`. Tested 5 frontier models (GPT-4.1-mini, GPT-5-mini, Gemini-3-flash, Claude-Haiku-4.5, DeepSeek-Reasoner). Built under PCS framework (Predictability, Computability, Stability), co-developed with practicing physicians.
Eval question: How sensitive are clinical LLM outputs to *non-adversarial* user-phrasing variation, and what evaluation design captures that sensitivity in a way that is decision-relevant for deployment? Specifically: can a single CUE-aligned benchmark distinguish models on benign elicitation stability — separate from adversarial robustness?
Methodology: Framework paper + benchmark release. Benchmark: 2,697 patient-authored queries from HealthCareMagic-100K (online medical-consultation platform), three-tier reference diagnosis sets. Perturbation: 7 factors annotated across 3 categories — content (symptom removal, objective-test removal, misleading beliefs), format (multiple-choice vs open-ended, binary agreement), tone (urgency/anxiety insertion, first-person reframing). Active perturbation = LLM-based **prompt neutralization** (single neutralized version per query, not multi-variant) targeting F1/F2/F5/F6/F7. Clinician validation: 2 clinicians edited reference sets on 50 cases.
Measurement: Four utility metrics computed against three-tier reference: **plausibility** (precision of proposed diagnoses inside the plausible set), **H-coverage** (recall of highly-likely diagnoses), **S-coverage** (recall of safety-critical diagnoses), **breadth** (size of differential). Sensitivity-to-elicitation = directional shift in those four metrics under prompt neutralization vs. original phrasing. Inter-rater proxy = commission/omission rate under both-annotator consensus criterion (no Cohen's kappa or ICC reported).
Key findings: Under prompt neutralization, models systematically increase plausibility while decreasing breadth — and H-coverage / S-coverage decline. The directional pattern is consistent across all 5 models tested (GPT-4.1-mini, GPT-5-mini, Gemini-3-flash, Claude-Haiku-4.5, DeepSeek-Reasoner), distributed across Figure 4 of the paper (no unified table). Clinician-edit agreement: agreed-upon removals occurred in only 2% of safety-critical sets and 8% of highly-likely sets, suggesting the constructed reference sets are stable. Bottom line: existing benchmarks under-report sensitivity to non-adversarial variation, and a CUE-aligned benchmark surfaces it.
Soft spots: (a) Single-author preprint, not peer-reviewed. (b) Source dataset (HealthCareMagic-100K) has no explicit PHI de-identification protocol stated; no IRB or consent language for benchmark use. The benchmark itself is CC BY 4.0, but the upstream provenance is a public Q&A platform of unclear legal status. (c) Inter-rater reliability is reported only as commission/omission rates on 50 cases — no Cohen's kappa, no ICC. (d) Single neutralized variant per query (not multi-variant) limits effect-size estimation. (e) **No prospective physician evaluation of model outputs** — clinician role was reference-set editing only. (f) No comparison vs existing perturbation benchmarks (e.g., MedQA noise variants).
Strengths: (a) Cleanly distinguishes adversarial robustness (already in KTP #9) from **benign elicitation stability** — a real gap in current Guidance. (b) Patient-authored queries instead of clinician-rephrased vignettes is a concrete, actionable design move. (c) Four utility metrics with explicit clinical-safety meaning (plausibility / H-coverage / S-coverage / breadth) — not aggregate accuracy. (d) Benchmark released under permissive license with public access. (e) Five-model coverage including reasoning models. (f) The 7-factor perturbation taxonomy (content/format/tone) is reusable independent of HCM-Dx.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Classification (differential diagnosis) and Conversational (patient-facing). Most relevant to `evaluation_methods_by_task.md` §Classification (input-sensitivity testing) and to a potential new sub-bullet under **KTP #9** distinguishing adversarial robustness from benign elicitation stability. Layer 2 (kit connection): No existing kit is patient-facing in the way HCM-Dx assumes. CUE adds an *input-side* dimension that the Discharge Letter Summarization kit (output-side) does not currently cover. If a future patient-facing kit is built, CUE is a candidate framework for input-sensitivity gating.
Verdict reasoning: Promote with caveats. The framework + benchmark is methodologically novel (benign elicitation stability is genuinely a gap in our KB) and the deep-dive resolved the three open verification questions: (i) benchmark size confirmed (2,697 queries, three-tier ref sets), (ii) HCM-Dx is permissively released — though source dataset PHI status is hand-waved, (iii) inter-rater is informal but transparent. The lack of prospective physician evaluation of LLM outputs is a real soft spot but doesn't disqualify a framework paper. Recommend Promote with explicit caveats in the card and a queued **minor update to G-02 (KTP #9)** introducing a benign-elicitation-stability sub-bullet — applied only if PQ-129 (CLEAR, humility deficit) is also promoted, since CLEAR provides the abstention-side complement.

Warner B, Grandhi RS, Kieffer M, Ouraq A, Panigrahi S, et al. Medmarks: A Comprehensive Open-Source LLM Benchmark Suite for Medical Tasks. arXiv:2605.01417. 2026 May 2.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01417
Overview: Open-source LLM evaluation suite for medicine (35 authors, lead Warner). 30 benchmarks spanning question answering, information extraction, medical calculations, and open-ended clinical reasoning. Evaluated 61 models across 71 configurations using verifiable metrics + LLM-as-Judge. A subset (Medmarks-T) is structured as reinforcement-learning environments. Frontier reasoning models (Gemini 3 Pro Preview, GPT-5.1, GPT-5.2) achieved highest performance. Notable methodology finding: smaller models (and Grok 4) susceptible to answer-order bias.
Eval question: Can a single open-source benchmark suite cover the breadth of medical-LLM evaluation tasks (QA, extraction, calculations, open-ended reasoning) with verifiable metrics, and what does cross-model comparison reveal about reasoning vs non-reasoning architectures and answer-order robustness?
Methodology: Aggregated 30 benchmarks across 4 task categories. Dual-track scoring: (i) verifiable metrics where ground truth is checkable (MCQ, calculations); (ii) LLM-as-Judge for open-ended reasoning. 61 models × 71 configurations (variant prompts, temperatures, model versions). Answer-order-bias probe: same MCQ presented with permuted option ordering. No physician validation reported.
Measurement: Per-benchmark verifiable scores (accuracy, F1) for closed-form tasks. LLM-as-Judge agreement scores for open-ended tasks. Answer-order-bias quantified per-model as score variance under option permutation. Token-efficiency reported as score/token-cost ratio.
Key findings: Frontier reasoning models lead overall (Gemini 3 Pro Preview, GPT-5.1, GPT-5.2). Most frontier proprietary models are significantly more **token-efficient** than open-weight alternatives — a deployment-cost finding. Medically fine-tuned models outperform their generalist counterparts. **Answer-order bias** is a measurable failure mode for smaller models and (notably) Grok 4, separate from accuracy. Specific exact deltas not in abstract; full paper required for per-benchmark numbers.
Soft spots: (a) Heavy reliance on LLM-as-Judge for open-ended reasoning (KTP #8 already cautions on this — bounded validity). No physician validation reported. (b) Benchmark aggregation can hide task-specific failures behind macro averages. (c) 30 benchmarks include known-contamination targets (USMLE-derived sets); no contamination-resistance discussion in abstract. (d) Single submission; no peer-review yet.
Strengths: (a) Open-source, reproducible — directly counters KTP #6 reproducibility concern. (b) Scale (61 models × 71 configs) is unusual for a single suite; cross-model comparisons benefit. (c) Answer-order-bias probe is a methodologically clean diagnostic that other suites usually omit. (d) Token-efficiency reporting is operationally useful for deployment-cost planning. (e) Medmarks-T as RL environments enables training-time use, not just eval-time.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Cross-task — QA, extraction, calculations, open-ended reasoning. Touches every kit. Layer 2 (kit connection): No specific kit overlap, but **answer-order-bias** finding is methodologically relevant to all classification-style sanity checks (option-permutation as a free probe). Could extend KTP #6 (benchmark reproducibility) with answer-order-bias as a within-benchmark diagnostic.
Verdict reasoning: Interesting, verify. Large-scale benchmark suites are useful but the abstract is light on physician validation and on contamination-resistance methodology — both of which are central to this repo's scope. The answer-order-bias finding is the most novel methodological contribution; would want to see exact per-model deltas before promoting. If user promotes, the value is in citing answer-order-bias as a within-benchmark diagnostic in KTP #6, not in adopting Medmarks scores as authoritative.

Guo KH, Yan C, Baidya A, Brown K, Goa X, Xiong J, Yin Z, Malin BA. CLEAR: Revealing How Noise and Ambiguity Degrade Reliability in LLMs for Medicine. arXiv:2605.01011. 2026 May 1.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.01011
Overview: Eight-author preprint introducing the **CL**inical **E**valuation of **A**mbiguity and **R**eliability (CLEAR) framework. Three-axis perturbation of medical benchmarks: (1) number of plausible answer options, (2) presence of ground-truth or abstention option, (3) semantic framing of answer options. Evaluates 17 LLMs across 3 benchmarks. Defines a new safety-relevant quantity — the **humility deficit** — as the gap between a model's ability to identify the correct answer and its ability to abstain when no correct answer is presented. Finding: humility deficit *worsens* with model scale.
Eval question: When standard medical benchmarks (USMLE-style MCQ) are perturbed along three real-world ambiguity axes (option count, abstention availability, semantic framing), how does LLM reliability degrade — and is the degradation pattern uniform across models, or does it scale adversely?
Methodology: Systematic perturbation of three benchmarks (specific names not disclosed in abstract — full paper required). For each base item, generate variants along three axes: (i) vary the number of plausible options (information dilution), (ii) toggle the presence of an explicit abstention option, (iii) reframe the same options semantically (e.g., negated phrasings). 17 LLMs evaluated. Define **humility deficit** = correct-identification accuracy − abstention-accuracy when no correct answer is present.
Measurement: Per-axis degradation curves: accuracy as a function of option count, accuracy with vs without abstention, accuracy under semantic reframing. Humility deficit = scalar per model. Trend analysis across model scale (parameter count or capability tier).
Key findings: Three core findings: (a) increasing the number of plausible answer options consistently degrades correct-identification accuracy across models, (b) presence of an explicit abstention option exposes a measurable performance gap, (c) **the humility deficit worsens with model scale** — larger models are not better at abstaining when they should, even though they identify correct answers more accurately. Authors frame this as 'scaling alone does not resolve LLM reliability issues' in clinical contexts.
Soft spots: (a) Specific model names and benchmark identities not in abstract — full-text required to assess external validity. (b) Three perturbation axes may interact non-orthogonally; main effects vs interactions not separated in abstract. (c) Humility deficit is a useful single-number diagnostic but the threshold at which it matters operationally (clinical-significance cutoff) is not proposed. (d) No physician validation of either the perturbations or the abstention quality. (e) Single preprint; no peer review.
Strengths: (a) **Humility deficit** is a clean, falsifiable, scaling-aware metric — directly fills a gap in our Guidance (current KTP #9 covers adversarial fragility but not abstention competence). (b) Three-axis design is methodologically rigorous. (c) 17-model coverage is large for a perturbation study. (d) Abstention competence is a safety-relevant property that current standards under-measure. (e) Counter-scaling result is non-obvious and policy-relevant.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Classification (MCQ-style) and Generation (input-side). Layer 2 (kit connection): The Lung Cancer Screening Eligibility kit's per-criterion structure could borrow CLEAR's abstention-presence axis as a sanity-check probe (does the model abstain when ineligible-by-rule?). KTP #9 sub-bullet candidate: distinguish adversarial robustness (PQ-77, PQ-59) from **abstention competence** (humility deficit, PQ-129) and benign-elicitation stability (PQ-125-FU CUE). All three are input-side properties, but operationally distinct.
Verdict reasoning: Promote. The humility-deficit construct is a measurable, named quantity that fills a real gap in the Guidance — abstention competence is currently invisible in our KTP table. The counter-scaling finding ('larger ≠ safer for abstention') is a non-obvious, methodologically important result. The lack of full-text-confirmed numbers is a limitation but the framework is independently useful even before per-model numbers are seen. Recommend Promote with **G-02 KTP #9 sub-bullet update** if user agrees.

Draelos RL, Afreen S, Blasko B, et al. Large language models provide unsafe answers to patient-posed medical questions. npj Digital Medicine. 2026. doi:10.1038/s41746-026-02428-5.
https://www.nature.com/articles/s41746-026-02428-5
Overview: Physician-led red-teaming study in npj Digital Medicine 2026 evaluating four publicly available chatbots (Claude/Anthropic, Gemini/Google, GPT-4o/OpenAI, Llama-3.0/3.1-70B/Meta) on patient-posed advice-seeking questions across primary-care topics (internal medicine, women's health, pediatrics). 222 patient-posed questions × 4 chatbots = 888 chatbot responses physician-rated for problematic-vs-unsafe content. Reports per-model rates and qualitative failure modes with potential for serious patient harm.
Eval question: When patients ask publicly available chatbots advice-seeking medical questions (the actual deployment context for direct-to-consumer LLM access), what fraction of responses are problematic vs outright unsafe, and is the failure rate uniform across leading models?
Methodology: Physician-led red-teaming. Curated 222 patient-posed advice-seeking questions covering internal medicine, women's health, pediatrics. Submitted each question to 4 chatbots → 888 responses. Physician adjudication along two severity tiers: 'problematic' (broader category) and 'unsafe' (subset with potential for serious harm). Specific IRR / Cohen's kappa not in abstract (full text required).
Measurement: Two binary outcome rates per model: problematic-response rate and unsafe-response rate. Qualitative failure-mode taxonomy from physician annotations. Comparison across the four models.
Key findings: Problematic-response rate ranges from **21.6% (Claude) to 43.2% (Llama)** — a ~2x spread across leading models. Unsafe-response rate ranges from **5% (Claude) to 13% (GPT-4o, Llama)** — a ~2.6x spread. Qualitative review surfaced responses with potential for serious patient harm (specific failure-mode categories require full text). Bottom line: patient-facing LLM deployment carries non-trivial unsafe-response rates even from frontier providers, and the choice of model materially changes risk exposure.
Soft spots: (a) 222 questions × 4 models is a moderate sample; per-subgroup (e.g., women's health vs pediatrics) power may be limited. (b) IRR / kappa not visible in abstract — adjudication reliability is the core methodological question for physician-rated red-team studies (cf KTP #7). (c) Models tested include older versions (GPT-4o, Llama-3.0/3.1-70B); rate generalization to current frontier (GPT-5, Claude 4.5, Llama 4) requires re-test. (d) 'Patient-posed' questions were curated, not sampled from real chatbot logs — selection effects possible.
Strengths: (a) Physician-led adjudication, not LLM-as-judge — rare and high-credibility for safety questions. (b) Two-tier severity (problematic vs unsafe) is operationally useful, separates 'suboptimal' from 'harm-potential.' (c) Cross-model comparison with concrete numerical spreads supports model-selection decisions in deployment. (d) npj Digital Medicine peer-reviewed venue. (e) Real patient-facing context, not benchmark-style USMLE.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Conversational / Patient-facing / Safety-critical. Layer 2 (kit connection): No existing kit is direct-to-consumer patient-facing, but PQ-130 is the strongest current empirical anchor for **safety-vs-effectiveness gating** (G-04) when a patient-facing kit is eventually built. Specifically reinforces: (i) safety must be a hard gate not a soft metric (G-04), (ii) per-model risk varies enough to make model choice a safety-relevant decision. Pairs naturally with PQ-77 (red-teaming taxonomy) and PQ-59 (adversarial hallucination) as the patient-facing-deployment empirical anchor.
Verdict reasoning: Promote. Physician-led red-teaming with concrete per-model rates is exactly the kind of empirical anchor G-04 (safety-vs-effectiveness) currently lacks for patient-facing deployment. The 5%–13% unsafe spread is a citable, decision-relevant number. The methodological soft spot (IRR not in abstract) is worth verifying in full text but doesn't disqualify a peer-reviewed npj DM paper with physician adjudication. Recommend Promote with citation in G-04 plus a cross-reference in evaluation_methods_by_task.md §Conversational under safety gating.
