Tao X, Dong X, Zhu Q, Zhou X. OEMA: ontology-enhanced multi-agent collaboration framework for zero-shot clinical named entity recognition. JAMIA Open. 2026 May 14;9(3):ooag049. doi:10.1093/jamiaopen/ooag049.
https://doi.org/10.1093/jamiaopen/ooag049
Overview: Full-text deep-dive on the OEMA paper after user 'more' decision on PQ-132. Three-agent zero-shot clinical NER framework — self-annotator (generates candidate examples via two-stage majority voting), discriminator (SNOMED CT ontology filter on token-level candidates), predictor (entity-type-description-enhanced final inference). Evaluated on three benchmarks (MTSamples — synthetic discharge summaries; VAERS — vaccine safety reports; I2B2 2010 — real discharge summaries) across three backbones (GPT-3.5-turbo, GPT-4.1, Gemini-2.5-flash). All four deep-dive questions from the original 'more' decision are now answered: exact F1 numbers per backbone × benchmark, SNOMED-CT discriminator ablation magnitude, English-only language coverage, and the comparison to supervised BioClinicalBERT.
Eval question: [Same as PQ-132, plus the four user deep-dive questions:] Exact F1 numbers per backbone × benchmark under both strict-match and relaxed-match? Magnitude of the SNOMED-CT discriminator ablation? Language coverage (English-only or multilingual)? Direct quantitative comparison to supervised BioClinicalBERT?
Methodology: Same three-agent pipeline as the original PQ-132 abstract, with the full-text adding: (1) self-annotator uses LLM zero-shot prompts + two-stage majority voting to generate self-labeled corpus; (2) discriminator retrieves K candidate examples, extracts top-level SNOMED CT concepts from test and candidate sentences, scores helpfulness, selects top-k; (3) predictor performs final NER using entity-type descriptions + selected few-shot examples. Benchmarks: MTSamples (3 entity types, synthetic), VAERS (4 entity types, vaccine-safety reports), I2B2 2010 (3 entity types, real discharge summaries). Supervised baseline: BioClinicalBERT trained on 500 examples.
Measurement: Exact-match F1 and relaxed-match F1 per benchmark per backbone. Ablation: F1 with vs. without the SNOMED-CT discriminator. Comparison vs. supervised BioClinicalBERT (500-example training set) under both match modes.
Key findings: **Exact-match F1 (best backbone = Gemini-2.5-flash):** MTSamples 64.7% / VAERS 56.9% / I2B2 67.9%. **Relaxed-match F1 (Gemini-2.5-flash):** MTSamples 87.4% / VAERS 77.2% / I2B2 **90.3%**. **Supervised BioClinicalBERT (500-example training):** exact 78.5%/66.8%/74.1%; relaxed 90.1%/80.2%/89.7%. **Headline result: zero-shot OEMA + Gemini EXCEEDS supervised BioClinicalBERT on I2B2 relaxed-match (90.3% vs 89.7%)**, but underperforms on exact-match across all three benchmarks. **SNOMED-CT ablation:** removing the discriminator drops F1 by ~5pp exact (e.g., MTSamples 51.6 → 46.5 on GPT-3.5) and ~3pp relaxed (MTSamples 82.0 → 78.9) — the paper concludes the SNOMED-CT component is 'a core driver of OEMA's semantic alignment,' not nominal. **Language coverage:** English-only; authors flag multilingual generalization as future work and note multilingual SNOMED CT versions as the path. **Cross-backbone consistency:** the same multi-agent + ontology pattern wins across GPT-3.5, GPT-4.1, and Gemini-2.5-flash — the framework, not the model choice, is doing the work.
Soft spots: (a) Strict-match F1 still trails supervised BioClinicalBERT by 10-14pp on MTSamples — the 'matches supervised' headline is **relaxed-match only**; cite carefully. (b) Authors acknowledge: 'lack independent human evaluation for intermediate reasoning steps (e.g., helpfulness scores)' — the discriminator's quality is benchmark-automatic, not clinician-judged. (c) 'SNOMED CT ontology extraction is performed by an LLM and treated as a black box' — the ontology call itself could fail silently. (d) 'Evaluation based on three relatively small datasets, which may not capture the full diversity of clinical language.' (e) 'Dependence on SNOMED CT constrains the framework's applicability to domains lacking comprehensive ontologies' — useless for ontologically-thin specialties. (f) No independent reproduction yet; single research group preprint-to-journal path.
Strengths: (a) **Quantitatively verified zero-shot parity with supervised BioClinicalBERT on I2B2 relaxed-match (90.3% vs 89.7%)** is a real result — not a methodological gesture. (b) **SNOMED-CT discriminator is load-bearing**, not decorative: ablation cleanly shows the ontology filter contributes ~5pp exact / ~3pp relaxed F1. (c) Three-backbone consistency (GPT-3.5 / GPT-4.1 / Gemini-2.5-flash) tells you the framework generalizes, which is the point. (d) Three diverse benchmarks (synthetic / safety-reporting / real EHR) — not benchmark-overfit. (e) JAMIA Open peer-reviewed venue. (f) Multi-agent + ontology-grounded pattern (self-annotator → discriminator → predictor) is a reusable template independent of NER specifics — applicable to any clinical extraction task with a relevant standardized ontology.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Extraction (central to evaluation_methods_by_task.md §2). Layer 2 (kit connection): **VAP Extraction** and **Delirium Extraction** kits are the natural candidates for borrowing the OEMA pattern as a reusable prompt-engineering reference (especially the ontology-discriminator step when SNOMED CT covers the target entities). The relaxed-match parity result also gives a citable anchor for Extraction §2: zero-shot clinical NER is no longer obviously inferior to fine-tuned BERT on relaxed-match metrics — choice between zero-shot and supervised becomes a strict-match-vs-relaxed-match decision rather than a categorical 'supervised wins.' Guidance update on Promote: add a bullet under Extraction §2 noting the multi-agent + ontology-discriminator pattern with the I2B2 relaxed-match parity number as the anchor, and the 'strict-match still trails' caveat as the bound.

Eval question: How rigorous is current LLM-as-judge practice in healthcare actually, and what governance framework should clinical deployments adopt to close the gap between 'use LLM-as-judge because it's scalable' and 'LLM-as-judge for high-stakes clinical evaluation requires structured safeguards'?

MedJUDGE framework synthesized post-hoc from gap analysis: risk-stratified by clinical risk tier; three pillars — (1) validity (does the judge measure what it claims?), (2) safety (does the judge fail safely?), (3) accountability (who is responsible when it fails?). (e) The MedJUDGE framework proposes risk-stratified tiers: high-stakes clinical tiers require independent expert panels + fairness audits + temporal-stability re-validation; lower-stakes tiers can lean more heavily on LLM-only judging with documented validity claims.

(e) Out-of-window for this scan (2026-04-03) — flagged for retroactive inclusion; should have been caught in scan #25 or #26.

(c) The **three-pillar (validity / safety / accountability) structure maps cleanly onto existing AI-governance frameworks** (NIST AI RMF, CHAI, FDA AI/ML guidance) — usable as a citable bridge in evaluation-planning documents. (d) **Risk-stratified tiering** matches the deployment-vs-research distinction that the user's evaluation philosophy already uses. Automated Safety Testing and Reporting Application (ASTRA) for Conversational Safety Monitoring of Generative AI Tools for Mental Health: Development and Validation Study. JMIR Mental Health. 2026 May 19. doi:10.2196/91367.
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42155999/
Overview: Methodology paper on **ASTRA** — an external safety-monitoring layer for conversational GenAI mental-health tools. ASTRA scans assistant outputs in therapeutic conversations and classifies risk-relevant content into eight predefined categories (the categories themselves are not enumerated in the abstract; full text required). Validation against 100 synthetic clinician-authored therapeutic conversations, with human-coder consensus ratings as the reference standard. Reports accuracy > 0.90 across all categories, sensitivity 0.55–1.00 (category-dependent), and Cohen's κ = 0.65–1.00 vs the consensus reference.
Eval question: Conversational mental-health LLM tools need ongoing safety monitoring that is independent of the assistant model itself. How can such a monitoring layer be validated against human coder consensus, and what level of agreement is achievable in a development/validation phase?
Methodology: (1) 100 synthetic therapeutic conversations authored by licensed clinicians, designed to reflect a range of risk-relevant behaviors across varying lengths and complexity. (2) Eight predefined risk-behavior categories (not enumerated in abstract — full text required). (3) Independent human coders score each conversation; consensus rating serves as reference standard. (4) ASTRA scores the same conversations; performance vs the consensus reference reported per category.
Measurement: Per-category accuracy, sensitivity, specificity (implicit), Cohen's κ vs human-coder consensus. Headline ranges reported: accuracy >0.90 (all categories); sensitivity 0.55–1.00 (varies by category); κ = 0.65–1.00 (varies by category). The κ-range upper bound at 1.00 means at least one category showed perfect agreement; the 0.55 sensitivity lower bound flags that some risk categories are harder for the monitor to catch.
Key findings: (a) **External safety-monitoring layer is feasible** — agreement vs human-coder consensus reached substantial-to-perfect levels (κ = 0.65–1.00) on a synthetic validation set. (b) **Per-category variance is wide:** sensitivity 0.55–1.00 means the system catches some risk types reliably and others poorly. (c) **Synthetic-conversation validation is a feasibility step, not a deployment-readiness gate** — authors explicitly call for 'evaluation using larger and real-world datasets.' (d) Methodology pattern (external monitor + human-coder consensus reference + per-category metrics) is reusable for any conversational LLM safety-monitoring layer.

(b) **Small sample (N=100)** — per-category power is limited; the wide sensitivity range (0.55–1.00) reflects both real category difficulty AND noise from small per-category cell sizes. (c) **Eight categories not enumerated in abstract** — full text needed to assess whether the category structure is clinically defensible. (d) **Development/validation study** by the same group building the system — independent third-party validation absent. (e) **No production deployment** — feasibility only. (f) The 0.55 sensitivity lower bound is the safety-relevant number: nearly half of the harder-category events would be missed if this threshold held in deployment.
Strengths: (a) **External safety-monitoring layer architecturally separate from the assistant model** is the right design pattern for conversational AI safety — independent monitor, independent reference standard. (b) **Human-coder consensus reference standard** with measured agreement is appropriate methodology for validating a monitor at the development stage. (c) **Per-category metrics reported** — not aggregate accuracy alone; the wide sensitivity range surfaces that some risk types are harder, which is decision-relevant. (d) **JMIR Mental Health peer-reviewed**. (e) Honest about limitations — authors flag the synthetic-data caveat and call for larger real-world evaluation.

Heckmann NS, Papoutsi DG, Barbieri MA, Battini V, Mølgaard SN, Schmidt SØ, Melskens L, Sessa M. Biomedical Large Language Models and Prompt Engineering for Causality Assessment of Individual Case Safety Reports in Pharmacovigilance. Pharmaceutical Research. 2026 May 23 (Online ahead of print). doi:10.1007/s11095-026-04121-4.
https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42174348/
Overview: Methodology paper evaluating biomedical LLMs combined with prompt-engineering strategies on the task of **causality assessment for Individual Case Safety Reports (ICSRs)** — the regulator-facing pharmacovigilance task of grading whether a reported adverse event is plausibly drug-caused. 150 ICSRs (140 from FAERS, 10 myocarditis/pericarditis from VAERS) graded by three biomedical LLMs (TinyLlama 1.1B, Medicine LLaMA-3 8B, MedLLaMA v20) against two human-expert reference algorithms (Naranjo, WHO-UMC). Agreement measured via **Gwet's AC1** and percent agreement.
Eval question: Pharmacovigilance ICSR causality assessment is high-volume, regulator-facing, and currently human-graded against the Naranjo or WHO-UMC algorithms. Can biomedical LLMs reach human-comparable agreement on the final causality classification — and which agreement metric is appropriate when prevalence is skewed across causality categories?
Methodology: (1) Dataset: 150 ICSRs total — 140 from FAERS (FDA Adverse Event Reporting System) + 10 myocarditis/pericarditis from VAERS. (2) Three LLMs tested: TinyLlama 1.1B (general), Medicine LLaMA-3 8B (biomedical), MedLLaMA v20 (biomedical). (3) Multiple prompt-engineering strategies including chain-of-thought (CoT) and direct prompts referencing the Naranjo and WHO-UMC frameworks. (4) Reference standard: human expert assessment via Naranjo + WHO-UMC algorithms. (5) Agreement metric: **Gwet's AC1** (chosen over Cohen's κ specifically because category prevalence is skewed — the paradox issue) plus percent agreement. (6) Qualitative error analysis on failure modes.
Measurement: Gwet's AC1 + percent agreement, per LLM × prompt-strategy combination vs human-expert consensus. Best combination: **Medicine LLaMA-3 8B with Naranjo-CoT prompt — 64% agreement on final causality classification.** Per-item granular agreement (the sub-questions within the causality framework, not just the final classification) shows weaker LLM performance.
Key findings: (a) **Best LLM-prompt combination reached 64% agreement** on the final causality classification — well below human inter-rater levels for the same task (~85% in prior literature). (b) **'Models showed weak performance on critical assessment items'** — sub-questions within the causality framework (the building blocks) were harder than the final classification, suggesting the model is partly guessing the final answer correctly while failing the reasoning underneath. (c) **'Frequent model failures included irrelevant responses'** — task-specification adherence is a failure mode even for biomedical-tuned models. (d) **Gwet's AC1 is the right metric for this task** — prevalence skew (most ICSRs cluster in 'possible' or 'probable' categories) would inflate Cohen's κ misleadingly. (e) Authors' bottom line: 'suboptimal for reliable causality assessment of ICSRs despite improvements over general-purpose models.'
Soft spots: (a) **Small sample (N=150)** for an agreement study — per-category power is limited, and the VAERS subset (N=10) is too small for VAERS-specific conclusions. (b) **64% agreement is not deployment-ready** — the paper is honest about this. (c) **Only three LLMs tested**, all open-source biomedical-tuned; GPT-4-class / Claude / Gemini frontier models not in the panel. (d) **No human-LLM disagreement adjudication** — when the LLM and the expert disagree, the paper doesn't analyze whether the LLM is sometimes 'right' for a different reason. (e) **Single-reference-standard study** — Naranjo and WHO-UMC are used but the human grader is a single source per item (no inter-rater for the reference itself reported in the abstract).
Strengths: (a) **Gwet's AC1 as the chosen agreement metric** is methodologically correct under skewed-prevalence and is itself the most KB-relevant contribution — directly informs the Statistician_Guide question of 'which agreement metric under prevalence skew.' (b) **Real-data evaluation** — FAERS + VAERS, not synthetic. (c) **Granular failure analysis** — separating final-classification agreement from item-level agreement is exactly the right diagnostic; the 'final answer right, reasoning wrong' pattern is a transferable finding. (d) **Honest 'suboptimal' framing** — no over-claiming. (e) **Peer-reviewed (Pharmaceutical Research)**.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Classification (causality categories: unrelated / unlikely / possible / probable / certain). Layer 2 (kit connection): No existing kit is pharmacovigilance-specific, but the **Gwet's AC1-vs-Cohen's-κ choice under prevalence skew** is directly relevant to the **Statistician_Guide** agreement-metrics section. Also worth a KTP #7 anchor: 'on a real-data, regulator-facing classification task, best LLM-prompt combination reached only 64% agreement vs human experts' — empirical evidence for the benchmark-vs-deployment gap on a non-MCQ task.

Kim JP. Augmenting Human Evaluation with LLM Judges: How Many Human Reviews Do You Need? arXiv:2605.16354. 2026 May 8.
https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.16354
Overview: Methodological preprint on **sample sizing for human + LLM-judge co-evaluation**. Treats the LLM judge as an auxiliary tool that augments — not replaces — human raters, and asks: given a target precision (variance budget) on the population-level evaluation metric, how many human reviews are actually needed when an LLM judge labels the rest? Uses a **doubly-robust estimator from the missing-data literature** that exploits the known missingness structure (human ratings are intentionally partial, with the LLM judge filling in). Provides asymptotic variance expressions analogous to the standard clinical-trial power calculation, allocating more human reviews where the LLM judge is least predictable. Author: Jane Paik Kim (Stanford affiliation not confirmed from the abstract page).
Eval question: When the cost-binding constraint on evaluation is human-reviewer time, and an LLM judge can label the rest at near-zero marginal cost, what is the smallest number of human reviews needed to estimate the evaluation target metric to a chosen precision? This is the explicit sample-sizing question — not 'is the LLM judge any good' (validation is a separate question) but 'given partial validation, how do I plan my human-review budget.'
Methodology: (1) Frame the problem as a two-stage sampling design: human reviews on a chosen subset, LLM-judge labels everywhere; estimator combines both. (2) Apply a doubly-robust estimator from the missing-data literature — robust to misspecification of either the LLM-judge labeling model OR the human-sampling mechanism, but not both. (3) Derive asymptotic variance of the estimator under known missingness structure. (4) Convert variance to sample size via standard power-calculation algebra. (5) Adaptive allocation: more human reviews where the LLM judge is least predictable (e.g., items the judge labels with low confidence or where judge-human disagreement is empirically high).
Measurement: Variance expressions and sample-size formulas for the population-level evaluation metric. No clinical worked example provided in the available abstract content — the paper is methodological, not applied.
Key findings: (a) **Doubly-robust co-sizing of human + LLM-judge labels gives sample-size formulas analogous to clinical-trial power calculations** — not a vague heuristic. (b) **Adaptive allocation** (more humans where judge least predictable) is the load-bearing efficiency move — variance reduction comes from spending the human-review budget where it matters most. (c) The doubly-robust property gives partial robustness to model misspecification in either the judge's labels or the human-sampling mechanism. (d) Domain-agnostic — no clinical example; the contribution is methodological transfer to the user's recurring sample-sizing questions.
Soft spots: (a) **No clinical worked example** — the paper does not demonstrate the method on a healthcare LLM evaluation, only the theory. Application requires the user to fill in the judge-prediction model + the sampling mechanism for their specific task. (b) **Doubly-robust ≠ unconditionally robust** — both the judge's labeling model AND the human-sampling mechanism can simultaneously be misspecified, in which case the estimator fails. The paper presumably bounds this; verification needed. (c) **Domain-agnostic framing** means clinical-specific concerns (label uncertainty, prevalence skew, multi-rater consensus) may need adaptation. (d) **Preprint, not peer-reviewed**. (e) **Affiliation unclear from the abstract page** — agent reported Stanford but the page did not confirm; downstream citations should not assume institutional anchor.
Strengths: (a) **Directly answers a question the user's evaluation philosophy keeps surfacing:** 'consensus and N are different problems' — this paper formalizes the trade-off as sample-size math, not slogan. (b) **Doubly-robust estimator** is the appropriate machinery from the missing-data literature; it is not ad hoc. (c) **Variance-based sample sizing** maps cleanly onto Statistician_Guide's existing sample-sizing approach (kappa CIs, Sim & Wright, Donner-Rotondi). (d) **Adaptive allocation** matches the user's existing instinct (e.g., disagreement-protocol calibration in the Nuclear Medicine kit). (e) **Open preprint** — code/equations available for adaptation.
Relevance: Layer 1 (task type): Generation / LLM-as-judge sample sizing — applies to any kit that uses LLM-as-judge as part of evaluation. Layer 2 (kit connection): **Statistician_Guide** is the natural home — under 'Sample sizing for LLM-as-judge augmented evaluation,' cite the doubly-robust two-stage approach as the principled sizing method when human review is the binding budget constraint. The Nuclear Medicine kit and any future kit using LLM-as-judge as a partial-rater are candidates. Concrete Guidance edit on Promote: add a Statistician_Guide section on co-sized human+judge evaluation, with PQ-140 as the methodological anchor and the caveat that the paper is domain-agnostic and requires clinical adaptation.

Methodology: Production shadow deployment from September 2025 onward. The full pipeline (detect → action) runs on real production data, with the comparator being the closest competitor ambient-scribe product. Hallucination taxonomy axes: Support (is the claim grounded in the transcript?) and Severity (would a clinician care?).
Measurement: (a) Total consultations processed: 150,000+ (cumulative since Sept 2025). (b) Detected hallucinations: 193,410. (c) Resolution rate: 81% of cases had ALL unsupported claims successfully resolved; aggregate hallucination-count reduction: 92.7%. (d) Hallucination-free-note rate: baseline ~33% → post-correction 86%; comparator 74%. (e) Hallucinations-per-note: 0.18 (Tortus) vs 0.38 (closest competitor) — ~2× advantage. Detection threshold and severity-axis cutoffs not disclosed.
Key findings: (a) **Real-world shadow deployment at 150K+ consultation scale** confirms that a detect-then-action pipeline can drive ambient-scribe hallucination-free rates from ~1/3 of notes to ~86% of notes. (b) **Competitor comparator (74% hallucination-free vs Tortus 86%)** is the rare vendor-comparator number in clinical AI engineering content. (c) **0.18 vs 0.38 hallucinations/note** is the headline efficacy ratio — a ~2× per-note reduction. (d) **Action is the load-bearing step**, not detection alone — the framing argument is that 'flagging is not enough; the pipeline has to correct.' (e) Honest failure-mode acknowledgment: temporal inconsistencies, intra-note contradictions, and missing context are explicitly named as still-undetected categories.
Soft spots: (a) **Vendor self-report with no independent audit** — the numbers are not independently verifiable. (b) **Detection ground truth = the detector itself** — without an independent reference standard, '193,410 detected hallucinations' is what the detector caught, not what existed. The 'fix rate' is therefore measured against the detector's own detections. (c) **No precision/recall on the detector** disclosed — false-positive rate of the detector is unknown. (d) **Severity axis and threshold not specified** — 'hallucination-free note' depends on what counts as a hallucination, and the cutoff isn't given. (e) **'Closest competitor' anonymized** — comparator identity not given, so the 74% / 0.38 numbers can't be cross-checked against the competitor's own claims. (f) **Marketing-adjacent venue** — the company is selling this; framing is honest but not neutral.
Strengths: (a) **Production-scale deployment numbers (150K+) are rare in clinical AI** — most published evaluation evidence is benchmark-scale or pilot-scale. (b) **Competitor-comparator with both numerator (86% vs 74%) and per-note rate (0.18 vs 0.38)** is unusually concrete for vendor content. (c) **Explicit acknowledgment of remaining failure modes** — the temporal-inconsistencies / intra-note-contradictions / missing-context list is candid. (d) **Methodology framing — detect-then-action, not just detect** — is the right pattern for clinical deployment. (e) **One of very few clinical-AI vendor engineering blogs publishing real metrics rather than testimonials** (alongside the existing Abridge PB-01 / PB-02 entries).
